## 1. Business‑level strategy basics

- Business‑level strategy explains how a firm positions itself to achieve competitive advantage in a specific industry or product market.
    
- Competitive advantage comes from the difference between the value created for customers VV and the total cost CC to produce that value.
    
- Managers choose a **strategic position** along two main dimensions: relative value created (high vs. low) and relative cost (high vs. low).

---

## 2. Generic business strategies and scope

- Two generic strategies: **differentiation** (higher value at similar cost) and **cost leadership** (similar value at lower cost).
    
- Scope of competition can be **broad** (industry‑wide) or **narrow** (focused on a particular segment or niche).
    

The four main business‑level strategies:

|Strategic position|Broad scope example|Narrow scope example|
|---|---|---|
|Differentiation|Broad differentiation (e.g., Apple in consumer electronics)|Focused differentiation (e.g., luxury niche brands)|
|Cost leadership|Broad cost leadership (e.g., Walmart)|Focused cost leadership (e.g., low‑cost niche discounters)|

- Firms that fail to commit to a clear position risk being “stuck in the middle” with no competitive advantage.
  

---

## 3. Differentiation strategy and value drivers

- Differentiation aims to increase the perceived value of products or services so that customers are willing to pay a premium price.
    
- The firm must ensure that the **economic value created** (V−C) is greater than competitors’, even after adding differentiation‑related costs.​
  

Key **value drivers**:

- Product features: superior design, performance, or quality.
    
- Customer service: responsiveness, customization, after‑sales support.
    
- Complements: additional products or services that increase total value (e.g., apps, accessories, ecosystems).
    

Risks in differentiation:

- Price–value gap becomes too large; customers no longer see the premium as justified.
    
- Differentiation features become less valuable due to changes in tastes or technology.
    
- Imitation narrows differentiation advantages over time.
    

---

## 4. Cost‑leadership strategy and cost drivers

- Cost leadership seeks to deliver acceptable value at the lowest possible cost, enabling lower prices and higher margins at a given price.
  
- Again, the goal is to achieve a larger V−CV−C than rivals, but primarily by driving **C** down rather than pushing **V** up.​

Key **cost drivers**:

- Cost of input factors: cheaper access to raw materials, labor, capital, or distribution.
    
- Economies of scale: lower unit costs as output increases, up to the minimum efficient scale.
    
- Learning‑curve effects: costs fall as cumulative output and experience grow.
    
- Experience‑curve effects: process innovations and new technologies shift the entire cost curve downward.
    

Risks in cost leadership:

- New technologies make existing cost advantages obsolete.
    
- Rivals successfully imitate cost structure, eroding margins.
    
- Excessive cost cutting undermines value (quality, service) and drives customers away.
    

---

## 5. Blue ocean strategy and value innovation

- A **blue ocean strategy** combines differentiation and low cost to create new, uncontested market space (“blue oceans”), instead of competing head‑to‑head in existing markets (“red oceans”).
    
- The core idea is **value innovation**: simultaneously increasing customer value and lowering costs, making competition less relevant.
    

Four‑actions framework (for value innovation):

- Eliminate: Which factors that the industry takes for granted can be removed?
    
- Reduce: Which factors should be reduced well below industry standard?
    
- Raise: Which factors should be raised well above industry standard?
    
- Create: Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?
    

Challenges and risk:

- Very hard to execute because differentiation and cost leadership pull the firm in opposite directions.
    
- If mismanaged, the firm ends up stuck in the middle—higher costs without enough additional value, or a commodity product without cost advantage.
    

---

## 6. Strategy, value–cost trade‑offs, and dynamics

- Strategic leaders must make **trade‑offs**: limited resources force choices between value drivers and cost drivers, and focusing on one usually weakens the other.
    
- Competitive advantage is not permanent; changes in technology, customer preferences, and rivals’ actions can erode any business‑level strategy.
    
- Managers need to monitor whether their strategic position is still unique and valuable or drifting toward the crowded middle of the value–cost space.
