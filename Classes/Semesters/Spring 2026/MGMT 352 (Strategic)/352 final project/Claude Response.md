
STRATEGIC ADVISORY REPORT

Airbnb, Inc.

Prepared for the Board of Directors

March 2026 | Confidential

Strategic Question:

How should Airbnb reposition itself publicly and operationally in response to the political narrative that short-term rentals worsen urban housing affordability, in order to reduce regulatory risk in major U.S. cities?

  

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Airbnb faces an existential regulatory threat in its most valuable markets. Major U.S. cities — New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Boston, and New Orleans — have enacted or are actively considering short-term rental restrictions premised on the belief that Airbnb worsens housing affordability. Whether or not this belief is empirically accurate is largely irrelevant to strategy: it is politically powerful, and political belief drives regulation.

This report recommends a Hybrid Proactive Partnership Strategy — combining credible public narrative reframing, city-by-city engagement, selective housing-ally investments, and product pivots toward longer-term stays. This is not a defensive posture, nor blind compliance. It is an assertive repositioning that forces cities to see Airbnb as an economic partner rather than a housing villain.

The window to act is narrow. New York City's Local Law 18 has already eliminated over 90% of Airbnb listings in the nation's most important market. If this model is adopted by Chicago, Los Angeles, and Boston, Airbnb will face a structural supply crisis in urban America within three years. The time to move is now.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  

II. SITUATION ANALYSIS: THE REGULATORY THREAT AND ITS BUSINESS CONSEQUENCES

A. Why Major Cities Are Airbnb's Most Valuable Markets

Airbnb's revenue is disproportionately concentrated in a small number of large, globally recognized cities. These markets are critical for three reasons: they generate the highest average daily rates (ADR), produce the greatest booking volume, and drive the network effects that make the platform self-reinforcing. In cities where hotel rates are extremely high, Airbnb's value proposition to guests is at its strongest — and therefore its take rate generates maximum revenue per transaction.

Major cities also carry outsized brand and perception value. What happens in New York shapes how hosts and guests worldwide perceive Airbnb's reliability as a platform. A successful presence in Manhattan signals legitimacy; a regulatory near-ban signals fragility.

B. The Regulatory Landscape

The regulatory environment facing Airbnb in U.S. cities has shifted dramatically since 2020. The following cases illustrate the severity and the pattern:

New York City

Regulation: Local Law 18 (2023) — primary-residence rule, host must be present, 2-guest maximum

Business Impact: 80-92% reduction in active listings; estimated $25M+ monthly revenue loss

San Francisco

Regulation: Registration requirement, primary-residence verification

Business Impact: 27% drop in availability; 53% fall in commercial listings; $5M/month host revenue lost

New Orleans

Regulation: Near-total ban on non-owner-occupied STRs in residential zones

Business Impact: Significant listing reductions in key tourist neighborhoods

Boston

Regulation: Permit requirements, owner-occupancy rules, rental limits

Business Impact: Reduced commercial listing viability; compliance costs deter new hosts

Los Angeles

Regulation: Home-sharing ordinance requiring primary residence, 120-night cap

Business Impact: Eliminated investor-owned listings; chilled host participation

C. The Regulatory Contagion Risk

The most dangerous dynamic is not any single city's regulation — it is the signaling effect. When New York City enacted Local Law 18, it demonstrated to every other major city that a near-total ban is politically viable. Cities that had previously considered moderate registration requirements now had a more aggressive template to copy. This creates a contagion effect: each new major-city ban lowers the political barrier for the next.

The pattern is self-reinforcing: housing advocates point to New York's law as a model; hotel lobbies fund similar campaigns in other cities; progressive mayors adopt housing-focused platforms that target STRs as visible, actionable targets. Airbnb is not just managing a regulatory problem — it is managing a spreading political narrative.

D. The Core Business Vulnerability

Airbnb's asset-light model — its greatest financial strength — is also its greatest regulatory weakness. Because Airbnb owns no property, it cannot buffer supply disruptions the way a hotel chain can. When a city bans entire-home listings, Airbnb's supply in that market disappears immediately and completely. There is no inventory to hold, no hotel rooms to keep operating, no long-term leases to draw revenue from.

The economic stakes are concrete: Airbnb's own commissioned research by Charles River Associates estimates that New York City's strict regulation has cost the city over $2.5 billion annually in shifted consumer spending — with the primary beneficiaries being incumbent hotel chains, not the housing market.

III. THE HOUSING AFFORDABILITY NARRATIVE: AN HONEST ASSESSMENT

A. What the Evidence Actually Shows

The empirical relationship between short-term rentals and housing affordability is genuinely contested. Airbnb and its allies point to the following facts:

- STR listings represent less than 0.5% of total housing stock in most major U.S. cities — far too small a share to be a primary driver of citywide rent increases.

- In New York City, after Local Law 18 eliminated over 90% of Airbnb listings, rents rose 8% over two years compared to 3.5% nationally — suggesting no affordability benefit from the ban.

- San Francisco's registration requirement reduced STR availability by 20-27%, yet long-term housing prices fell only marginally, consistent with the view that STRs are not a primary affordability driver.

- Hotels — not housing advocates — are the primary lobbying force behind the most aggressive STR restrictions, raising questions about whose interests are actually being served.

However, Airbnb must be honest about the evidence on the other side:

- Research from Harvard Business Review found a positive correlation between neighborhood-level Airbnb listings and asking rents, suggesting localized price pressure even if citywide effects are small.

- Entire-home listings operated by commercial investors — as opposed to individual homeowners — do meaningfully remove units from the long-term rental market.

- In hyper-supply-constrained markets, even marginal reductions in available units can have outsized price effects.

B. Why the Narrative Matters More Than the Evidence

The strategic reality Airbnb must accept is this: in democratic urban politics, narrative power matters as much as empirical accuracy — often more. Housing affordability is the defining political issue in every major U.S. city. It is visceral, personal, and electoral. A politician who targets Airbnb can claim visible, decisive action on a problem that voters care deeply about — regardless of whether that action will actually reduce rents.

The hotel industry and housing advocacy community have been highly effective at framing Airbnb as a villain in this story. The image of apartment buildings converted entirely to tourist use — displacing real residents — is politically powerful even if it describes only a fraction of actual Airbnb listings. Airbnb has so far been reactive and defensive in its public response. That must change.

IV. STRATEGIC OPTIONS: EVALUATION AGAINST BINDING CONSTRAINTS

Four strategic options were evaluated against five binding constraints: legal compliance, brand protection, revenue growth, platform feasibility, and speed of impact.

OPTION A: Proactive City Partnership

Core Approach: Data-sharing, compliance technology, housing fund contributions in exchange for workable regulatory frameworks

Legal Compliance: High | Brand Protection: High | Revenue Growth: Medium | Feasibility: High | Speed: Medium

OPTION B: Long-Term Stay Pivot

Core Approach: Shift focus to 28+ day stays and Airbnb-friendly apartments, reducing STR exposure

Legal Compliance: High | Brand Protection: Medium | Revenue Growth: Medium-High | Feasibility: High | Speed: High

OPTION C: Housing-Ally Model

Core Approach: Per-booking fees to housing funds, transparent data, co-design regulation with cities

Legal Compliance: High | Brand Protection: Very High | Revenue Growth: Medium | Feasibility: Medium | Speed: Low-Medium

OPTION D: Defensive Compliance

Core Approach: Comply city-by-city, lobby and litigate, wait for political shifts

Legal Compliance: High | Brand Protection: Low | Revenue Growth: Low | Feasibility: High | Speed: N/A (reactive)

Option A: Proactive City Partnership — Detail

This approach involves Airbnb proactively approaching city governments before regulations are enacted — offering a compliance technology platform, real-time data sharing, automatic removal of unlicensed listings, and contributions to city housing funds — in exchange for regulatory frameworks that permit responsible STR activity to continue. The key logic is preemption: by giving cities the tools they claim to want (transparency, enforcement, housing contributions), Airbnb reduces the political pressure for blunt bans.

Strengths: This approach treats cities as partners rather than adversaries, builds durable regulatory relationships, and demonstrates that Airbnb can be a responsible actor. It also gives cities a face-saving alternative to outright bans. Weaknesses: Some cities are not interested in compromise — particularly where hotel lobbying is strong or where housing politics demand visible action regardless of effectiveness.

Option B: Long-Term Stay Pivot — Detail

Airbnb's 28+ day stay products — often classified as medium-term rentals — fall outside most STR regulations. A strategic pivot toward this segment, combined with an expanded Airbnb-friendly apartment program (where Airbnb partners directly with building owners to offer compliant longer-stay units), would structurally reduce Airbnb's exposure to the regulatory category that is under attack. COVID-19 validated this market: Airbnb's long-stay segment became its fastest-growing during 2020-2021.

Strengths: Directly removes revenue exposure from the most regulated product category; already proven at scale; appeals to a different and growing segment of demand (remote workers, relocating employees). Weaknesses: Lower average daily rates than short-stay urban listings; puts Airbnb in competition with furnished apartment platforms it was not originally designed to compete against.

Option C: Housing-Ally Model — Detail

The most ambitious option involves Airbnb formally repositioning itself as a partner in solving the housing crisis — not just defending against the narrative. This would include a per-booking fee directed to a dedicated affordable housing fund, co-designing regulatory frameworks with housing advocates and city governments, publishing comprehensive housing impact data, and publicly committing to eliminating commercial investor listings that are not owner-occupied. Airbnb has already taken a small step in this direction with its $1 million partnership with Neighborhood Housing Services of NYC, announced in September 2025.

Strengths: If credible and sustained, this option has the potential to fundamentally alter Airbnb's political positioning — from housing villain to housing partner. Weaknesses: Requires significant financial commitment; risk of appearing cynical if not backed by genuine operational changes; may alienate commercial hosts who generate disproportionate revenue.

Option D: Defensive Compliance — Detail

The current default posture: comply city-by-city, remove illegal listings under duress, lobby against proposed restrictions, challenge the most egregious regulations through litigation, and wait for political conditions to shift. This is not a strategy — it is the absence of one. New York City's outcome under this approach is the clearest possible evidence of its failure. Defensive compliance cedes narrative ground, invites further restriction, and positions Airbnb as resistant and unaccountable.

V. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATION: THE HYBRID PROACTIVE PARTNERSHIP MODEL

The Core Recommendation

Airbnb should pursue a Hybrid Proactive Partnership Strategy that combines elements of Options A, B, and C into a coherent, phased approach. No single option is sufficient on its own. The recommendation has three integrated pillars:

PILLAR 1 — NARRATIVE OFFENSE: REFRAME THE STORY

Airbnb must stop playing defense on the housing affordability narrative and go on offense. The data is on its side — but data alone does not win political arguments. The company needs a sustained, credible public communications strategy built around three reframes:

- From "Airbnb removes housing" to "Airbnb enables housing stability" — amplify the story of individual homeowners using host income to pay their mortgages, avoid foreclosure, and afford the cities where they live.

- From "Airbnb vs. cities" to "Airbnb vs. hotel lobbies" — make the hotel industry's role in funding STR restriction campaigns a central part of the public debate. The Charles River Associates study provides the evidentiary foundation for this argument.

- From "we follow the rules" to "we helped write good rules" — shift from reactive compliance to proactive co-authorship of workable regulatory frameworks.

This is not spin — it is making the honest, evidence-based case more loudly and more strategically than Airbnb has to date. The company's September 2025 poll showing 81% of New Yorkers believe the city is going in the wrong direction on housing — after two years of Local Law 18 — is precisely the kind of data that should anchor a public narrative campaign.

PILLAR 2 — CITY PARTNERSHIP PROGRAM: OPERATIONALIZE THE RELATIONSHIP

Airbnb should launch a formal City Partnership Program targeting the ten U.S. cities at highest regulatory risk within the next 12 months. The program should offer each city:

- A real-time data-sharing dashboard giving city officials visibility into listing volume, host primary-residence status, revenue, and tax collection.

- Automatic removal of listings that fail city registration requirements — removing the enforcement burden from city government.

- A dedicated per-booking contribution to city affordable housing funds, structured as a voluntary commitment in partnership markets (approximately $1-2 per booking).

- A Responsible Hosting Framework co-designed with each city that distinguishes between individual homeowners (politically protected, economically valuable) and commercial investors operating multiple properties (the appropriate target of regulation).

The strategic logic is to make moderate regulation the path of least resistance for city politicians. By giving them a working partnership they can point to as a policy achievement, Airbnb reduces the political incentive for blunt bans. Cities that adopt the Partnership Program framework should receive public recognition and Airbnb's active support for defending the framework against hotel lobbying pressure.

PILLAR 3 — PRODUCT DIVERSIFICATION: REDUCE STR CONCENTRATION RISK

Airbnb should accelerate its longer-term stay product as a structural hedge against STR-specific regulation. Specific actions include:

- Expand the Airbnb-Friendly Apartments program — partnerships with building owners to offer 28+ day stays in purpose-built or designated units — targeting 50,000 additional units in regulated markets within 24 months.

- Create a differentiated "Primary Home" category in the platform that makes host owner-occupancy status visible to guests and city regulators alike — building trust through transparency and creating a politically defensible tier of supply.

- Develop a regulatory impact modeling tool for hosts — helping individual homeowners understand their local regulatory environment and comply proactively, reducing the number of non-compliant listings that give cities a pretext for sweeping action.

PHASE 1: FOUNDATION (Months 1-6)

PHASE 3: CONSOLIDATION (Months 19-36)

Key Actions: Publish year-1 housing impact results; expand program to 20+ cities; advocate for Local Law 18 reform in NYC using partnership model data; position as national STR regulatory standard.

Success Metrics: NYC listing recovery; no new major-city bans in partnership markets; long-stay revenue reaches 25%+ of urban revenue.

VII. RISKS AND LIMITATIONS

Risk 1: Cities That Are Not Open to Compromise

In some markets — particularly where hotel lobbying is dominant or where housing politics have become deeply entrenched — no partnership offer will be sufficient to prevent restrictive legislation. Airbnb must accept that it will lose some markets regardless of strategy. The goal of the Hybrid Partnership Model is not to win every city — it is to contain the contagion and win enough cities to maintain a viable urban supply base.

Risk 2: Commercial Host Alienation

The Responsible Hosting Framework explicitly distinguishes between individual owner-occupiers and commercial multi-property investors. Commercial hosts generate disproportionate revenue per listing but are the appropriate political target of regulation. Airbnb must be honest with this segment that the future of the platform in regulated markets requires their activity to be reduced or repositioned. This will create short-term host tension and possible revenue loss in the transition period.

Risk 3: Credibility of the Housing-Ally Commitment

If Airbnb's housing fund contributions and partnership investments are perceived as cosmetic — insufficient relative to the company's revenues and the scale of the housing crisis — they will invite more cynicism and more regulation rather than less. The company generated approximately $11 billion in revenue in 2024. A $1 million partnership with a New York housing nonprofit, while a positive first step, does not credibly signal institutional commitment at the scale the political environment demands. The per-booking contribution structure in the City Partnership Program must be meaningful enough to be taken seriously.

Risk 4: Coordination Across 10,000+ Hosts

Because Airbnb does not control its hosts, it cannot mandate their compliance with the Responsible Hosting Framework. The company can incentivize, educate, and surface compliant behavior — but individual hosts operating outside the framework remain a reputational and regulatory liability. The Primary Home listing tier addresses this partially by creating a visible distinction, but enforcement remains imperfect.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

VIII. CONCLUSION

Airbnb's regulatory challenge in major U.S. cities is not primarily a legal problem — it is a political and narrative problem. The housing affordability crisis is real, politically powerful, and will not go away. Airbnb's choice is not whether to engage with it, but how.

The Hybrid Proactive Partnership Strategy outlined in this report is not a capitulation to the regulatory narrative. It is a sophisticated, evidence-based counteroffensive that reframes Airbnb's identity, builds durable city relationships, and structurally reduces the company's dependence on the specific product category — entire-home short-term rentals operated by commercial investors — that provides the most compelling political justification for bans.

The alternative — continued reactive, defensive compliance — has already produced its results in New York City: a 90% listing reduction, no measurable improvement in housing affordability, and a regulatory template that is now being studied by every other major city in the country. Airbnb cannot afford to let that template spread.

The board is advised to authorize the Hybrid Proactive Partnership Strategy as Airbnb's primary regulatory response posture for the 2026-2029 planning horizon, with Phase 1 actions beginning immediately.
