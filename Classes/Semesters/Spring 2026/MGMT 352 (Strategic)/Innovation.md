## 1. Competition driven by innovation

- Innovation is presented as the main force that shifts competitive advantage over time, often more important than static industry structure.
    
- The chapter uses Netflix as a running example: from DVD‑by‑mail to streaming to original content, showing how continuous innovation reshapes an industry.[](https://studylib.net/doc/27850855/strategic-management-ise-frank-t-rothaermel-6--2024-mcgra...)​
    
- The speed of innovation is accelerating (digital tech, AI, platforms), which compresses product life cycles and forces faster strategic responses.

## 2. The innovation process (the “4 I’s”)

You learn a step‑by‑step view of how innovation happens.

- Idea: novel concepts often based on research, creativity, or problem‑solving.
    
- Invention: transforming an idea into a new product, process, or technology (often protected via patents or trade secrets).
    
- Innovation: commercialization of an invention—bringing it to market and capturing value.
    
- Imitation: rivals copy or improve the innovation, which erodes the original innovator’s advantage over time.
    

Entrepreneurship is defined as taking economic risk to innovate, create new products or services, or open new markets.

---

## 3. Strategic and social entrepreneurship

- Strategic entrepreneurship: using innovation to pursue competitive advantage and growth at the business level (new products, markets, or business models).
    
- Social entrepreneurship: creating new organizations or initiatives that solve social problems while also pursuing financial sustainability.
    
- The chapter highlights that entrepreneurs can work inside existing companies (**corporate entrepreneurship / intrapreneurship**), not just in start‑ups.

## 4. Innovation and the industry life cycle

The chapter links innovation to the **industry life cycle**, and to the idea of “crossing the chasm.”

Typical stages:

- Introduction: small market, early adopters, high uncertainty; product and business model experimentation.
    
- Growth: rapid demand increase, entry of competitors, emergence of standards.
    
- Shakeout: demand growth slows; weaker firms exit; price competition increases.
    
- Maturity: market is saturated, demand mainly replacement/repeat; process innovation and efficiency dominate.
    
- Decline: demand falls due to new technologies or changing preferences; firms choose to exit, harvest, maintain, or consolidate.
    

Crossing the chasm:[](https://christianleaders.org/mod/page/view.php?id=83968)​

- There is a gap between early adopters and the early majority; many innovations fail to “cross” from niche enthusiasts to a mainstream market.
    
- Managers must adapt product, marketing, and business model as the customer segment changes over the life cycle.

## 5. Types of innovation

Rothaermel classifies innovation along two dimensions: **technology (existing vs. new)** and **markets (existing vs. new)**.

- Incremental innovation: builds on existing technologies and target markets (e.g., annual smartphone upgrades).
    
- Radical innovation: uses new technologies to create new markets or dramatically change existing ones (e.g., first smartphone, biotech drugs).
    
- Architectural innovation: reconfigures existing technologies to create new products or markets (e.g., moving desktop components into laptops or tablets).
    
- Disruptive innovation: starts with simple, low‑cost offerings for low‑end or new customers, then improves and moves upmarket, displacing incumbents (e.g., early digital photography, streaming vs. cable TV).
    

The chapter emphasizes that incumbents are often good at incremental innovation but vulnerable to disruptive entrants.

## 6. Platforms, ecosystems, and network effects

A major new theme in this edition is **platform‑based business models**.​

- Platforms: business models that create value by enabling direct interactions between different user groups (e.g., buyers and sellers on Amazon, riders and drivers on Uber).[](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/study-notes/27627355)​​
    
- Network effects: the value of a product or service increases with the number of users (e.g., more users on a social network make it more valuable to each user).​[](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/study-notes/27627355)​
    
- Platforms often sit at the center of **ecosystems** of complementors (app developers, content providers, hardware partners).[](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/study-notes/27627355)​​
    

Strategic implications:

- Platforms compete not only on product features but on ecosystem size and engagement.​[](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/study-notes/27627355)​
    
- Once a platform reaches critical mass, network effects can create very strong, self‑reinforcing advantages, but also risks of winner‑take‑all dynamics and regulatory scrutiny.[](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/study-notes/27627355)​​

## 7. Key takeaways for managers

- Innovation is not a one‑off event; it is a **process** and a **capability** that must be built and aligned with business strategy.
    
- Entrepreneurial orientation (innovativeness, proactiveness, risk taking) at both the firm and individual level supports successful innovation and growth.
    
- Managers must recognize where their industry is in the life cycle, which type of innovation they are pursuing, and whether a platform or pipeline model fits their opportunity.
