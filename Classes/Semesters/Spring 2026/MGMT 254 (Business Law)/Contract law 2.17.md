
Offer and Acceptance

- Offer = present intent to enter a **binding** deal, communicated, and specific to an individual (or sufficiently definite offerees).​
    
- Acceptance = unqualified “yes” to the offer’s terms by words, writing, or conduct.​
    
- Grocery store example: placing goods on the belt = offer by conduct; cashier scanning and stating a total = acceptance.​
    
- No duty to accept: offeree always has the right to refuse or ignore an offer.​
    
- Extreme price example ($2 million for a used textbook): still a valid offer; just unlikely to be accepted.​
    
- Agreement = offer + acceptance; once formed, parties are bound (if all other elements exist).​
    

Invitations to Negotiate (Not Offers)

- Ads, flyers, catalogs, price lists, MLS listings, and online listings are usually invitations to negotiate, not offers, because they are not specific to a particular individual.​
    
- Pizza ad (medium pepperoni $6.99): not an offer; customers make the offer when they order.​
    
- Cruise ad ($399 Mexican Riviera): not an offer; traveler makes the offer by contacting the seller and requesting that deal.​
    
- Home sale listing with price sign: invitation to negotiate; seller under no obligation to accept full listing price.​
    
- Store mannequin or display price tag: invitation to negotiate; store can refuse to sell the display item.​
    
- Shelf price vs register price: shelf tag is invitation; customer offers at register; store’s higher scanned price is a counteroffer the customer can accept or reject.​
    

Counteroffers

- Counteroffer = response that changes any material term (e.g., price); it kills the original offer.​
    
- Example: seller offers book for $200 (real offer because specific to you); buyer responds “I’ll give $10” → counteroffer eliminates the $200 offer.​
    
- After a counteroffer, original offeror is free; they do not have to go back to original terms.​
    
- Negotiations often move back and forth (e.g., settle at $90 for the book) before final acceptance creates an agreement.​
    

Genuineness of Assent

- Requires parties to clearly agree on the same thing; terms must be definite enough for a judge to know what was bought/sold.​
    
- Vague promises (“I’ll sell you a book,” when there are multiple possible books) fail for lack of genuineness of assent.​
    
- Example: “I’ll sell you my car” when seller owns more than one car is too vague unless clarified which car.​
    
- Genuineness of assent is also the label used in the transcript for clarity of the bargain; later in the course it usually includes issues like mistake, fraud, duress, etc.​
    

Consideration – General Rule

- Consideration = each party must give something of legal value or give up a legal right.​
    
- Under positive law: “peppercorn” rule – anything with economic value as small as a mustard seed is enough.​
    
- Examples of positive-law consideration:
    
    - Seller gives up a book; buyer gives money.​
        
    - Pizza (good) for $6.99 (money).​
        
- Under equity, giving up a legal right can be consideration even if no tangible economic benefit is received (forbearance).​
    

Consideration – Forbearance Examples

- Car accident settlement: wrongdoer pays money; injured party gives up their right to sue in writing → consideration on both sides.​
    
- Smoking-for-money example: 19‑year‑old agrees not to smoke for six months; uncle promises $1,000.​
    
    - Nephew gives up legal right to smoke → consideration under equity.​
        
    - Uncle must pay if condition is satisfied.​
        

Lack of Consideration – Noncompensated Secrecy Agreement

- David Copperfield secrecy form: workers agree not to reveal trade secrets.​
    
- Worker gives up right to talk/post about methods → consideration under equity on worker’s side.​
    
- Copperfield’s company provides nothing: no money, no tickets, no lunch, no wages tied to this agreement.​
    
- Result: form is not a legally enforceable contract due to lack of consideration from the company.​
    

Secrecy Agreement as Enforceable Employment Contract

- If a regular employer (like the convention center) requires employees to sign a secrecy or trade secret policy as a condition of employment, the analysis changes.​
    
- Employee gives up right to disclose information.​
    
- Employer provides wages, benefits, and continued employment in return.​
    
- In that scenario, there is consideration on both sides; contract is enforceable.​
    

Charitable / Nonprofit Pledges – Exception to Consideration

- General rule: promises without consideration are not enforceable.​
    
- Exception: promises (pledges) to charities, churches, universities, or certain government bodies may be enforceable even without consideration if used for the stated purpose.​
    
- Church organ pledge: member promises $3,000 for a new organ; gets no benefit, but many courts enforce the pledge if church actually buys the organ for that project.​
    
- If funds are diverted to another purpose (e.g., building expansion instead of organ), donor can reclaim the money; using funds contrary to stated purpose is a breach.​
    
- University scholarship funds: donor gives money subject to specific restriction (e.g., home economics scholarship only).​
    
    - If major disappears and funds are not used as promised, heirs may sue to recover unused funds (plus growth) due to breach.​
        

United “$0 Tickets” Mistake Example

- Online “$0 ticket” fares: website display is invitation to negotiate; buyer makes the offer when they “buy” at $0.​
    
- United’s acceptance plus traveler’s obligation to pay $0 means airline receives no consideration.​
    
- No consideration to United → no legally enforceable contract, assuming no fraud or misrepresentation.​
    
- Airline often honors such tickets anyway for goodwill and PR reasons, but law does not require performance.​
    

Competent Parties – General Rules

- Competent parties requirement: each party must have legal capacity (not a minor, not mentally incompetent, not so intoxicated that they cannot understand the transaction).​
    
- Incompetent persons can usually enter contracts, but they (not the other side) have a right to disaffirm within a reasonable time after becoming competent.​
    
- If the other side did not and could not reasonably know of incompetence (e.g., online transaction), disaffirmance may not be allowed.​
    

Intoxication and Contracts

- Intoxicated person can form a contract; it is voidable at their option if the other party knew or should have known of their incapacity.​
    
- Bar example: extremely drunk professor trades a valuable 1976 Corvette for a pitcher of beer in front of friends.​
    
    - Offer: car for pitcher of beer.​
        
    - Consideration: beer vs car.​
        
    - Other party clearly sees intoxication.​
        
    - Contract is voidable by the intoxicated party; they can later demand the car back by returning the price of the beer.​
        
- E‑commerce example: drunk person accidentally adds an extra decimal point on eBay and offers $500 instead of $50.​
    
    - Seller has no reason to know buyer is intoxicated.​
        
    - Buyer cannot disaffirm simply due to intoxication.​
        

Minors and Necessaries

- Minors (under 18) lack full capacity; contracts with minors are generally voidable at the minor’s option until a reasonable time after turning 18.​
    
- Exception: “necessities of life” – minors must pay reasonable value for necessities (housing, food, medical care, and education).​
    
- Non‑necessaries (e.g., video games) can be disaffirmed: minor returns the item and can claim a full refund of the price paid, even if the item’s current value is much lower.​
    
- Store selling non‑necessaries to minors assumes the risk that the minor may later rescind and demand all money back.​
    

Minors – Fraudulent Misrepresentation of Age

- If a contract or order form clearly states “if you are under 18, your parents must sign,” a minor’s signature alone can be fraudulent.​
    
- In such cases, minor may be held personally liable for the obligations after turning 18 because of their misrepresentation.​
    

Unlawful Purpose

- Contracts must have **lawful** purpose; courts will not enforce agreements to commit crimes or torts.​
    
- Extortion example: person with a gun demands $500 for a Diet Pepsi; “agreement” is not enforced because coercion and illegality.​
    
- Buying alcohol for minors: agreement to purchase alcohol for a 16‑ or 17‑year‑old is illegal; if “buyer” takes their money and runs, neither side can enforce the bargain.​
    
- Murder-for-hire (“hit” contract): courts will not help either party; no recovery of partial payments for illegal activity.​
    
- Prostitution example: three underage girls provide illegal services; check bounces; they cannot recover in court due to illegality and criminal nature of the transaction.​
    

Marriage as a Contract

- Marriage certificate is a contract document identifying “contracting parties.”​
    
- Historically governed by general contract principles; today it is heavily modified by specific statutes and positive law.​
    
- Example used to illustrate that marriage is often the most “expensive” contract many people enter.​
    

Statute of Frauds – Overview

- Statute of Frauds (49 of 50 states; Louisiana is the main outlier) requires certain contracts to be in writing to be enforceable.​
    
- Rationale: prevent fraud and perjury in high‑risk contexts where oral evidence is unreliable.​
    

SOF: Sale of Goods ≥ $500

- Contracts for the sale of “goods” (tangible personal property, not attached to land) for $500 or more must be in writing.​
    
- Goods examples: soda, paper, ties, glasses, books, desks not affixed to realty.​
    
- Real property (land and fixtures attached for their useful life) is not a “good” under this rule.​
    
- Some states have raised the threshold (e.g., $5,000), but Indiana still follows $500.​
    

SOF: Contract Not Performable Within One Year

- Any contract that cannot, by its terms, be fully performed within one year from the date of making must be in writing.​
    
- Applies regardless of dollar amount when performance necessarily exceeds one year.​
    
- Example: agreement today to mow lawns each summer for two summers (e.g., total of 15 months from now) must be written.​
    
- If performance could be completed within a year, even if unlikely, SOF may not apply; focus is on possibility, not actual performance.​
    

SOF: Sale of Land

- Contracts for the sale of land or any interest in land must be in writing.​
    
- Historical reason: farmers were being defrauded by false testimony claiming an oral sale of land.​
    
- Modern application: any transfer of real estate ownership or major land interest requires a written, signed contract.​
    

SOF: Promise to Pay the Debt of Another (Suretyship)

- Co-signer or guarantor promises to pay another’s debt if the primary debtor defaults.​
    
- Because the co-signer often receives little direct benefit, the promise must be in writing to be enforceable.​
    
- Includes promises to personally pay debts of an estate beyond estate assets; writing is necessary to impose personal liability.​
    

SOF: Promise in Consideration of Marriage

- “Promise in consideration of marriage” mainly refers to dowry or other financial arrangements conditioned on marriage.​
    
- Examples in American custom: bride’s family pays for wedding, groom’s family pays for rehearsal dinner or related expenses.​
    
- These are customary, not legally required, unless put in writing; then they become enforceable contracts.​
    

Equity: Detrimental Reliance and Part Performance

- Equity is used when one or more legal elements is missing, but justice requires relief.​
    
- Detrimental reliance (promissory estoppel): if a party reasonably relies on a promise to their detriment and suffers loss, a court may award reliance damages even without a full contract.​
    
- Part performance: if a party has already partially performed under an otherwise unenforceable contract, equity may require payment at least for the part performed.​
    
- Typical use: to avoid unjust enrichment when one side has received benefits and the other has relied or performed.​
    

Oral Contracts and Enforceability

- Oral contracts can be fully enforceable if they do not fall under the Statute of Frauds categories.​
    
- Example: actor orally agrees to provide services and breaches; damages (e.g., $1.6 million) can be awarded if contract was for services lasting less than one year.​
    
- If the contract is for sale of goods over the threshold or otherwise within SOF, lack of writing can bar enforcement.​
