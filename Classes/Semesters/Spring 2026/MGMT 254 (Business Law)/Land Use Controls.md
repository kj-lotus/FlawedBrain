## Zoning

Zoning is the government's power to restrict how land can be used, justified by the goal of **uniformity of land use** — keeping similar uses in the same areas. Types of zoning restrictions include:​

- **Use categories**: agricultural, single-family residential, multi-family residential, commercial, industrial (unrestricted)
    
- **Physical restrictions**: setbacks (distance from property lines), building height limits, architectural style requirements (e.g., Western motif, Bavarian look)
    
- **Operational restrictions**: no drive-thrus in residential-adjacent areas, limits on unrelated occupants in a single-family home
    

**Real example from Indiana:** A BP/McDonald's franchise operator needed a zoning variance because local ordinance prohibited drive-thru restaurants near residences (due to pollution, noise, and litter concerns). The professor recommended a **variance** (a one-parcel exception) rather than a full **rezoning**, which would have opened the area to undesired uses like pawn shops.​

**West Lafayette example:** A local ordinance limits no more than **3 unrelated persons** in a single-family dwelling; a landlord was sued and ordered to pay $52,000 in litigation costs when 5 students resided in one unit, and the landlord subsequently sued the tenants for breach of their lease contract.​

---

## Variances

A **variance** is a subsequent, post-zoning modification granted for a single parcel. Examples discussed include:​

- Indoor/outdoor amusement parks
    
- Outdoor restaurant seating
    
- Drive-thru facilities
    

Variances allow flexibility without rezoning an entire area, protecting neighboring properties from undesired land uses.​

---

## Non-Conforming Uses

A **non-conforming use** is a pre-existing use that no longer conforms after new zoning is applied. Key rules:​

- The use **may continue** (immediate government seizure would require constitutional compensation)
    
- It **cannot be improved, expanded, or changed** to a different use
    
- If the property is **destroyed** (e.g., by fire), it cannot be rebuilt
    
- If the use is **discontinued** for even a reasonable period, it is considered **abandoned** and cannot resume​
    

**Example:** A laundromat operating in a neighborhood later rezoned residential can stay, but cannot renovate, expand, or convert to a law office — and cannot close temporarily without risking loss of non-conforming status.​

---

## Restrictive Covenants

**Restrictive covenants** are private restrictions placed by a property developer into deeds when land is subdivided, binding all subsequent owners. Purpose: maintain property values and uniformity across a subdivision. 

- No fences over 3'6" high (to preserve river views)
    
- No re-subdivision of parcels
    
- Minimum square footage requirements (e.g., 1,800 sq. ft. minimum)
    
- No detached structures (sheds, etc.)
    
- No antennas over 6 feet; rooftop A/C units must be fenced
    
- No solar panels on street-facing side of home
    
- No basketball hoops on front of home
    
- Second-story windows cannot overlook a neighbor's backyard​
    

**Critical practical tip:** Always ask specifically about **restrictive covenants** when purchasing property — they are separate from zoning and can significantly restrict use even on commercially-zoned land. The professor described nearly purchasing 10 acres zoned "commercial" in Madera, CA, only to discover a covenant restricting it to single-family residential.​

**Covenant enforcement:** Covenants run with the land. Violations can be enjoined by neighboring property owners. After typically **20–30 years**, covenant parties may vote to amend or remove restrictions.​


## Nature & Types of Property Rights

Real property includes land, buildings, and anything permanently attached to the land (fixtures), as well as **air rights** (above ground) and **subsurface rights** (minerals, oil, gas below). Ownership is not absolute — it is a **bundle of rights** that can be divided in many ways.​

**Fixtures** are personal property that becomes real property once permanently attached; courts apply the "intent of the parties" test — was the item meant to stay permanently?[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​

---

## Ownership Interests

- **Fee simple absolute**: The most complete form of ownership — the owner holds full rights indefinitely and can sell, lease, or devise it by will
    
- **Life estate**: Ownership only for the duration of someone's life; at death, property passes to a designated **remainderman**
    
- **Future interests (reversion/remainder)**: The original grantor or a third party receives the property after a life estate ends[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​
    

---

## Concurrent Ownership

|Type|Key Rule|Survivorship?|
|---|---|---|
|**Joint tenancy**|Equal shares, created simultaneously|Yes — survivor takes all|
|**Tenancy in common**|Unequal shares allowed; heirs inherit|No — passes to heirs|
|**Community property**|Applies in 9 states; marital property split equally|Varies by state|

A joint tenant can **sever** joint tenancy by selling their share, converting it into a tenancy in common.[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​

---

## Easements

An **easement** is the right to use another's land for a specific purpose. Types:[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​

- **Easement appurtenant**: Benefits a neighboring parcel (e.g., a right-of-way driveway across a neighbor's land); transfers automatically with the land
    
- **Easement in gross**: Personal right not tied to land ownership (e.g., a utility company's right to run power lines)
    
- **Easement by prescription**: Arises from long-term, open, and continuous use without permission — similar to adverse possession
    

---

## Adverse Possession

To claim **adverse possession** (acquiring title by use), the claimant must show possession that is:[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​

1. **Actual and open** — visible, not hidden
    
2. **Notorious** — the owner should be aware
    
3. **Hostile** — without the owner's permission
    
4. **Continuous** for the statutory period (varies by state: 5 years in Nevada; 20 years in Maine)
    
5. **Exclusive** — not shared with the public
    

> **Textbook Example:** A married couple in Boulder, Colorado — a judge and an attorney — met all elements of adverse possession and won a quiet title claim over land the true owners purchased to build a future retirement home. The Colorado legislature subsequently amended state adverse possession law in response.[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​

---

## Nuisance

A **private nuisance** is an unreasonable interference with another's use and enjoyment of their property. A **public nuisance** affects the community at large. Courts balance the utility of the defendant's conduct against the harm caused.[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​

> **Classic example used in textbooks:** A neighbor operating a noisy factory next to a residential subdivision may be liable for private nuisance. The court may issue an injunction or award damages.

---

## Eminent Domain

The government may take private property for **public use** but must pay **just compensation** (5th Amendment). This is called a **taking**. A **regulatory taking** occurs when government regulation so severely restricts use that it effectively takes the property without physically seizing it.[](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/545/469/)​

## Landmark Case: _Kelo v. City of New London_ (2005)

**Facts:** The city of New London, CT, condemned privately owned homes in a neighborhood to transfer to a private developer for an economic revitalization project.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelo_v._City_of_New_London)​

**Issue:** Does taking private property to transfer to another private party for economic development qualify as "public use" under the 5th Amendment?[](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/545/469/)​

**Held:** Yes — 5–4 decision. The Supreme Court ruled economic development benefits the public broadly and qualifies as public use.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelo_v._City_of_New_London)​

**Significance and Backlash:** This case is a cornerstone in business law textbooks. After the ruling, **47 states strengthened eminent domain protections**, and **12 states amended their constitutions** to prevent government from using eminent domain to transfer land to private developers. Ironically, the development project was never built — the land sat vacant.[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelo_v._City_of_New_London)​

> **Textbook feature:** Anderson's specifically features this case under the heading **"Putting the Brakes on Eminent Domain"**.[](https://www.sweetstudy.com/files/andersonsbusinesslawandthelegalenvironmentcomprehensivevolume23rded-3354941.pdf)​

---

## Transfer of Real Property

- **Warranty deed**: Seller guarantees clear title and will defend against any claims
    
- **Quitclaim deed**: Transfers only whatever interest the seller has — no guarantees
    
- **Recording statutes**: First party to record a deed generally prevails in disputes over the same property
    
- **Title insurance**: Protects the buyer if title defects are discovered after purchase[](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/montgomerycollege-masterybusinesslaw2/chapter/real-property/)​

#  Environmental Law — Expanded Textbook Notes

## Overview of Federal Statutes

Anderson's instructs students to be familiar (not deeply expert) with the following federal statutes:​

---

## Clean Air Act (CAA)

Originally enacted in **1963** and significantly expanded in 1970 and 1990, the CAA authorizes the EPA to set **National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS)** for six major pollutants (particulate matter, ozone, CO, SO₂, NO₂, lead). It regulates both **stationary sources** (factories, power plants) and **mobile sources** (cars, trucks).​

> **Landmark Case: _West Virginia v. EPA_ (2022):** The U.S. Supreme Court limited the EPA's authority under the CAA to mandate "generation shifting" (forcing power plants to switch from coal to cleaner energy), invoking the **major questions doctrine** — Congress must speak clearly when delegating enormous economic/policy decisions to agencies.​

---

## Clean Water Act (CWA)

Prohibits discharge of pollutants into **navigable waters of the U.S.** without an **NPDES permit** (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System). The Act covers industrial discharges, agricultural runoff, and wetlands protection.

---

## RCRA — Resource Conservation and Recovery Act

Governs the generation, transportation, treatment, storage, and **disposal of solid and hazardous waste** from "cradle to grave". Facilities generating hazardous waste must track it through a manifest system.[](https://www.justia.com/law-schools/justia-case-law-outlines/environmental-law-cases-outline/)​

> **Case: _City of Chicago v. Environmental Defense Fund_:** A resource recovery facility's management activities were excluded from RCRA's most stringent regulation, but the **toxic ash it generated was not** — illustrating that RCRA's hazardous waste provisions apply even if the facility itself is excluded.[](https://www.justia.com/law-schools/justia-case-law-outlines/environmental-law-cases-outline/)​

---

## CERCLA — Superfund

The **Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act** (1980) authorizes EPA to clean up contaminated sites and recover costs from **Potentially Responsible Parties (PRPs)**. CERCLA liability is:[](https://pages.mtu.edu/~drshonna/cm4310/lectures/chapter3.pdf)​

- **Strict** (no negligence required)
    
- **Joint and several** (one party can be held for all costs)
    
- **Retroactive** (applies to past disposal even before CERCLA existed)
    

PRPs include: current owners/operators, past owners, generators of waste, and transporters.[](https://www.justia.com/law-schools/justia-case-law-outlines/environmental-law-cases-outline/)​

## Textbook Feature: _"The Leaking Barrels GE Sold"_

Anderson's features a case study titled **"The Leaking Barrels GE Sold"** involving General Electric's liability under CERCLA for hazardous waste contamination — illustrating how a company that sold or disposed of waste decades earlier can still be held liable under the law's retroactive provisions.[](https://www.sweetstudy.com/files/andersonsbusinesslawandthelegalenvironmentcomprehensivevolume23rded-3354941.pdf)​

> **Case: _U.S. v. Monsanto Co._:** Off-site waste generators were found liable under CERCLA when they shipped hazardous substances to a disposal facility and those substances were later found at the contaminated site.[](https://www.justia.com/law-schools/justia-case-law-outlines/environmental-law-cases-outline/)​

> **Case: _Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. U.S._:** The Supreme Court clarified that a party must take **intentional steps** to dispose of a hazardous substance to qualify as an "arranger" under CERCLA, and that **apportionment** of costs is proper when there is a reasonable basis for separating each party's contribution to the harm.[](https://www.justia.com/law-schools/justia-case-law-outlines/environmental-law-cases-outline/)​

## Land Use Controls — Additional Textbook Detail

## Zoning Enforcement Sequence

When a property owner violates zoning, the enforcement escalates as follows:​

1. **Fine** issued by the governmental entity
    
2. **Injunction** (court order to stop the activity)
    
3. **Contempt of court** and **additional fines** if they continue
    
4. **Jail** for continued contempt
    

## Non-Conforming Uses — Rules at a Glance

|Situation|Result|
|---|---|
|Building destroyed by fire|**Cannot** be rebuilt|
|Owner wants to expand|**Cannot** enlarge|
|Owner wants to renovate exterior|**Cannot** improve|
|Use changes (e.g., laundromat → law office)|**Not allowed** — must remain same use|
|Owner vacates for a "reasonable time"|Use considered **abandoned**; cannot resume ​|

## Restrictive Covenants vs. Zoning

|Feature|Zoning|Restrictive Covenants|
|---|---|---|

|Feature|Zoning|Restrictive Covenants|
|---|---|---|
|Created by|Government|Private developer/seller|
|Enforced by|Government (fines, injunctions)|Neighboring property owners (lawsuit)|
|Can restrict|Land use categories|Nearly anything (colors, sq footage, sheds, windows, basketball hoops)|
|Changed by|Rezoning/variance process|Vote of property owners after 20–30 years ​|
