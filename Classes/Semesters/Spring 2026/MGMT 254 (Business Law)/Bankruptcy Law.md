## 1. Big picture of bankruptcy

- Bankruptcy = legal process to forgive or restructure debts a debtor cannot pay.
    
- U.S. has a federal bankruptcy statute dating back to 1898; it has been amended many times but is still the basic framework used today.
    
- Historically, under English law and early U.S. practice, people who could not pay debts could be put in **debtors’ prison**, where conditions were harsh and many died within a few years.     
    
- Modern policy: allow an honest but unfortunate debtor to get a “fresh start” rather than lifetime punishment
    

Why it matters:

- Businesses frequently file bankruptcy; employees, vendors, and lenders may all be affected.
    
- Individuals may file, and also may be affected if their employer files; loss of wages can trigger their own financial crises.
    

Key players:

- **Debtor**: person or business that owes money.[
    
- **Creditor**: person or business to whom money is owed.
    
- **Trustee in bankruptcy**: government‑appointed person who takes control of debtor’s estate, enforces the automatic stay, decides what to liquidate, and negotiates with creditors.

## 2. Types of bankruptcy discussed

Most common types covered:

- **Chapter 7 – Liquidation**
    
    - Goal: collect non‑exempt assets, sell them, pay creditors by priority list, then discharge remaining dischargeable debts.
        
    - Available to individuals, partnerships, corporations.
        
    - For a corporation, Chapter 7 effectively ends the entity. For individuals, they can start over but can file only every seven years (under this description).
        
- **Chapter 11 – Reorganization**
    
    - Used by businesses and individuals with significant debt.
        
    - Trustee works with creditors to reduce debts and extend time for payment, consistent with a business plan for recovery.
        
    - If reorganization fails, trustee can push debtor into Chapter 7 liquidation.
        
- **Chapter 13 – Extension of time to pay**
    
    - Only for individuals, not corporations.
        
    - Conceptually similar to Chapter 11: reorganization and extension of time to pay, with some debt discharged and some paid over time.
        
    - Designed for individuals with smaller total debt and fewer creditors, making administration more efficient
        

Other chapters briefly noted:

- Municipalities (e.g., Detroit) may be able to file depending on state law; Indiana’s constitution does not allow Indiana municipalities to file.
    
- Farmer bankruptcies exist but are not covered in detail.

## 3. Priority of payment and wage example

When “the boss” files bankruptcy, the question is who gets paid first.

Priority list (simplified from the transcript):

1. **Domestic support obligations**: alimony, maintenance, child support.
    
2. **Costs of administering the bankruptcy**: fees and expenses of trustee, attorneys, accountants; if they were not paid, professionals would not help run the system.
    
3. (Intervening categories exist; focus in transcript is on wages at number 4.)
    
4. **Claims for wages, salaries, commissions**:
    
    - Maximum priority amount an individual can get is $12,475.
        
    - These statutory amounts are updated every three years for inflation.
        

Illustrative scenario:

- Employee earns $10,000 per month and continues working for three months after employer stops paying, because quitting would disqualify them from unemployment benefits and they need a job while job hunting.
    
- Employer owes them $30,000 when bankruptcy is filed.
    
- Even if there is enough money to pay wage claims to the statutory maximum, each employee can receive only up to $12,475; the rest (about $17,525 in this example) is lost.
    
- Lower‑priority unsecured creditors (“all other unsecured creditors”) are paid only after all higher priority claims are paid in full, which often never happens; frequently the case never reaches general unsecured creditors
    

Key principle:

- Each class in the priority list must be paid in full before any lower class gets anything.

## 4. Chapter 7: liquidation and exemptions

Chapter 7 is called liquidation, but an individual does not lose **everything**, because of exemptions.     

Examples using the professor as hypothetical debtor:

## Vehicle exemption

- Debtor can keep equity in one car up to $3,675.
    
- If car is worth $2,000 and fully paid off, debtor keeps it (value below the exemption).
    
- If car is worth $23,675 and fully paid off:
    
    - Trustee would sell the car.
        
    - Debtor keeps $3,675; trustee uses $20,000 to pay creditors.
        
- If the car is worth $23,675 but debtor owes $20,000 to the lender:
    
    - Equity = $3,675, which is within exemption.
        
    - As long as debtor continues making payments, trustee will not take the car.
        
    - After bankruptcy, if debtor stops paying, the secured creditor can repossess using its lien.
        

## Home (homestead) exemption

- If home is worth $200,000 and fully paid off, trustee would want to take up to $177,025 in equity (given the figures in the transcript) to pay creditors.     
    
- More realistic example: home worth $200,000 with $180,000 mortgage:
    
    - Equity = $20,000.     
        
    - Debtor can keep up to $22,975 in homestead equity, so trustee will not seize the home.     
        
    - Debtor keeps the home as long as mortgage payments continue.     
        

## Household furnishings

- Individual item cap: no single furnishing can have present market value over $575 or trustee can seize it.     
    
- Total cap for furnishings: $12,250 (used value).     
    
- Value is what the item is worth used, not what it cost new.
    
    - Example: couch bought for $1,500 two years ago might only be worth $100–$500 now.     
        

## Jewelry

- Debtor can keep up to $1,550 in jewelry (used value, e.g., gold content).     
    

## Tools of the trade

- Purpose: avoid making debtor unemployable.     
    
- Debtor may keep tools needed to work (e.g., laptop and basic office items for attorney; tools for electrician, plumber, carpenter) up to $2,300 used value.     
    

## Wildcard / other property

- Debtor can keep $1,225 in any property plus up to $11,500 beyond other categories.     
    

Approximate total:

- With all categories combined, debtor could keep roughly $53,000 worth of property and still get a Chapter 7 discharge.     
	    

Conclusion:

- Chapter 7 is a **partial** liquidation; debtor is not totally destitute afterward.

## 5. Nondischargeable debts (do not go away)     

Certain debts survive bankruptcy discharge:

1. **Recent taxes**
    
    - Taxes within three years of filing remain payable.     
        
2. **Money or property obtained by false pretenses or fraud**
    
    - Example: overstating income on a credit card application (e.g., reporting $122,000 instead of actual $120,000 salary) can make that credit card debt nondischargeable.     
        
3. **Willful and malicious injuries**
    
    - Intentional torts such as battery; intentionally injuring someone or their property.     
        
    - Judgments from such intentional acts remain after bankruptcy, including wrongful‑death type civil judgments.     
        
4. **Debts from driving while intoxicated (DWI)**
    
    - Injuries or property damage arising from drunk driving involving cars, trucks, boats, motorcycles, RVs remain, beyond insurance.     
        
    - Example in transcript: if debtor caused accident while intoxicated and insurance is insufficient, remaining judgment cannot be discharged.     
        
5. **Alimony, maintenance, child support**
    
    - Domestic obligations are nondischargeable (consistent with their top priority status).     
        
6. **Unscheduled debts**
    
    - If debtor fails to list a creditor on the schedules, that debt may not be discharged.     
        
    - Example: debtor has seven credit cards but lists only six; the omitted card is an unscheduled debt and continues after bankruptcy.     
        
7. **Debts arising from fraud or breach of fiduciary duty**
    
    - Includes embezzlement or theft from an employer or another entrusting party; judgments for those amounts remain.     
        
8. **Government fines and penalties within three years**
    
    - Similar treatment to taxes.     
        
9. **Educational loans**
    
    - Federal student loans are (in the transcript’s description) nondischargeable for seven years from when the loan obligation arises.
        
    - Historical context: reform to stop professionals from discharging large federally insured student loans right after graduate school and leaving government to pay.     
        
    - Example: loans taken freshman–senior year plus two years of grad school (six years), then one year of repayment = seven years; earliest loans might be dischargeable, later ones still owed.     
        
10. **Certain prior bankruptcy debts with reaffirmation**
    
    - If debtor **reaffirms** a debt (puts in writing, filed with the court, agreeing to pay despite discharge), it remains enforceable.     
        
    - Example: parents loan money for education, are listed as creditors and would get nothing, but threaten to write child out of their will unless the child reaffirms the debt. Child might voluntarily reaffirm and then must pay.     
        
11. **Luxury goods / cash advances shortly before filing**
    
    - Presumption against discharge for luxury purchases or cash advances on credit cards within a short period before filing; the statute sets dollar thresholds and time windows.

## 6. Chapter 13: requirements and use     

Who can file Chapter 13?

- Must be an **individual** (not a corporation or partnership).     
    
- Must have **regular income**.     
    
    - Example: a substitute teacher working sporadically is not considered to have regular income and so cannot file, even though an individual.     
        
- Debt limits (as stated in the transcript):
    
    - **Secured debt** (debts with liens, like mortgages and car loans) must be less than $1,149,525.     
        
    - **Unsecured debt** (no lien, such as credit cards, student loans, IOUs) must be less than $383,175.     
        
- These numbers are odd amounts because they are periodically adjusted for inflation.     
    

Example of eligibility:

- Professor notes that he is an individual with regular income and his secured and unsecured debts are below the thresholds.     
    
- He therefore could file either Chapter 7 (liquidation) or Chapter 13 (extension of time), while Chapter 11 would be possible but more expensive and unnecessary given his debt level.

## 7. Involuntary bankruptcy and automatic stay     

## Involuntary bankruptcy

- Creditors can sometimes force a debtor into bankruptcy (usually Chapter 7 or 11), called **involuntary bankruptcy**.     
    
- Conditions:
    
    - Debtor is **not paying debts as they come due** (e.g., not paying credit cards, mortgages, car loans, hiding the car from repossession).     
        
    - Threshold amount: if debtor has fewer than 12 creditors, a creditor (or group) with claims totaling at least $14,425 can file an involuntary petition.     
        
- They cannot force you into bankruptcy just because your **liabilities exceed your assets**; many students with large loans have that situation but pay on time and cannot be forced into bankruptcy.     
    
- If creditors force a debtor into bankruptcy while the debtor is current on payments, the debtor can contest and may recover:
    
    - All costs, reasonable attorney fees, and any damages suffered due to the improper involuntary filing.     
        

## Automatic stay

- Filing any bankruptcy petition triggers an **automatic stay**.     
    
- Automatic stay = freeze on creditor actions:
    
    - Creditors must stop lawsuits, garnishments, and collection efforts.     
        
    - Secured creditors must stop repossessing cars or foreclosing on homes.     
        
- Trustee now controls what happens to debtor’s assets and creditor claims.

## 8. Preferential transfers (90‑day rule)     

Definition and purpose:

- A **preferential transfer** occurs when, within 90 days before filing, a debtor pays some creditors but not others in a way that is not in the ordinary course of business.     
    
- Goal: prevent debtor from favoring some creditors over others just before filing, and ensure fairness via the statutory priority system.     
    

Example scenario:

- Corporation (treated as an artificial person) is in financial trouble.     
    
- In the last 90 days before filing, it:
    
    - Pays utilities (water, gas, sewer, electricity) fully to keep operating.     
        
    - Pays a weekly vendor heavily so the vendor continues deliveries.     
        
    - Pays another vendor that it buys from only every six months little or nothing.     
        
    - Possibly falls behind on employee commissions or wages.     
        
- When bankruptcy is filed, trustee can demand that parties who received **preferential** payments in the prior 90 days return the money to the estate to be redistributed according to the priority scheme.     
    

Impact on individuals:

- Suppose an employee on commission was paid $10,000 (or $20,000 or $30,000) in the last 90 days, while the company did not pay others it owed.     
    
- Trustee may demand that employee return those commissions as preferential transfers.     
    
- If employee cannot repay, they may need to borrow money or even consider personal bankruptcy, especially if the employer goes out of business under Chapter 7 and the employee loses their job.

## 9. Part 2 examples: choosing chapters via scenarios   
These examples illustrate how chapter choice depends on income, debt type, debt amounts, and goals.

## 9.1 Kim Basinger (actor)       

Facts:

- Major debt: $9.5 million judgment for backing out of an oral agreement to star in a film.       
    
    - Verbal contract for services (acting) not subject to UCC sale‑of‑goods writing requirement.       
        
    - Production company proved the movie could be made within a year, satisfying statute of frauds without writing.       
        
- Other debts: mortgages on multiple homes, car loans, credit card debts, etc.       
    
- Total assets: about $5 million; total debts greatly exceed assets.       
    

Chapter 13?

- Initially: she has irregular acting income and no regular job → fails the “regular income” requirement.       
    
- Even if she gets a sitcom role and has regular income, the unsecured judgment of $9.5 million is far above the Chapter 13 unsecured debt cap of $383,175.       
    
- Likely her secured debts on homes and cars also exceed $1,149,525, further disqualifying her.       
    

Chapter 7 or 11?

- She **can** file Chapter 7 or Chapter 11 (the “7–11” mnemonic).       
    
- But she likely **does not want** Chapter 7 because:
    
    - She spends about $43,000 a month (including $7,000 on pet care).       
        
    - Under a Chapter 7 liquidation, she would be left with only approximately $40–50k of exempt assets (car equity, small home equity, jewelry, etc.), losing most of her lifestyle and property.       
        

Preferred outcome:

- She will most likely file Chapter 11:
    
    - Trustee negotiates with creditors to reduce the judgment and extend time to pay.       
        
    - Creditors might accept, for example, $5 million paid over five years instead of $9.5 million, because in a Chapter 7 they might receive at most around $500,000–$1 million.       
        
    - Given potential income of $3 million per movie or hundreds of thousands per sitcom episode, she can realistically pay reduced amounts over time.       
        
- If her income later collapses (quits or is fired from sitcom), trustee can convert the case to Chapter 7 and liquidate, but she wants to avoid that.       
    

## 9.2 Family business (corporation)       

Facts:

- Third‑generation family corporation; you are president, most relatives work there.       
    

Chapter 7?

- If the business files Chapter 7, it liquidates and almost all relatives lose their jobs.       
    
- President likely wants to avoid family backlash and job losses.       
    

Chapter 13?

- Not possible because the debtor is a corporation, and Chapter 13 is only for individuals.       
    

Preferred Chapter 11:

- File Chapter 11 to reorganize:
    
    - Trustee negotiates with vendors and creditors to reduce claims and extend payment periods.       
        
    - Goal is to keep the business operating so it can pay reduced debts over time and keep employees working.       
        
- If the reorganization fails, trustee can later force conversion to Chapter 7; president can blame the “government trustee” rather than taking personal blame for liquidation.       
    

## 9.3 Purdue student’s parents (homeowners)       

Facts:

- Parents of an undergraduate student.       
    
- Debts: home mortgage (possibly a second mortgage), car loans, credit cards.       
    
- They have lost their job, so they no longer have regular income.       
    

Chapter 13?

- No regular income → cannot file Chapter 13.       
    

Choices: Chapter 7 vs. 11

- If they have **significant equity** in home and assets, they likely do not want Chapter 7 because those assets could be taken and sold.       
    
- If they expect to regain employment soon, Chapter 11 reorganization may work:
    
    - Trustee develops a plan; creditors agree to take less and accept payment over time.       
        

Alternative scenario (housing crash):

- Suppose it is 2009 in Florida or California, their home value has dropped from $200,000 to $100,000, and they owe more on the home than it is worth (underwater).       
    
- They now have far more debt than equity and may not have to give up anything in Chapter 7 because there is no equity to capture.       
    
- In that situation, Chapter 7 liquidation is attractive to discharge debts.       
    

## 9.4 Purdue student (undergraduate)       

Typical cases:

- Students sometimes inquire about bankruptcy to escape student loans, but generally:
    
    - Their debt is not large enough to justify bankruptcy.       
        
    - Student loans survive because of the seven‑year nondischarge rule.       
        

Special accident scenario:

- Undergraduate junior with minimal assets at the time.       
    
- Minimum auto insurance coverage in the state is $50,000.       
    
- Student causes accident resulting in $300,000 in injuries/property damage.       
    
    - Insurance pays $50,000; $250,000 remains as judgment.       
        
    - Court sets interest at 10%, adding $25,000 per year in interest.       
        
- Assets: very limited; eligible exemptions allow student to keep around $50,000 worth of property (car, household items, etc.) and they probably have less than that.       
    

Effect of Chapter 7:

- If the accident was mere **negligence** (no intoxication, not intentional):
    
    - The $250,000 judgment is dischargeable in Chapter 7.       
        
    - Student keeps all their modest assets under exemptions; large judgment disappears.       
        

Limitations:

- Chapter 13 is unavailable because the student has no regular income, even though unsecured debt (including $250,000 judgment) is under the $383,175 cap.       
    
- Student would not want Chapter 13 anyway; they do not want to pay a large portion of that debt over time.       
    

Nondischargeable versions:

- If the student was **intoxicated** at the time, the $250,000 judgment is nondischargeable under the DWI rule; it follows them after bankruptcy.       
    
- If the student **intentionally** runs over the professor in a crosswalk out of anger:
    
    - Suppose judgment of $350,000 for willful and malicious injury.       
        
    - That debt is nondischargeable in any form of bankruptcy and will follow the student.       
        

## 9.5 United Airlines (large corporate debtor)       

Facts:

- Hypothetical example; not asserting actual financial condition.       
    
- You are CEO of United Airlines; the company faces financial difficulties.       
    

Chapter 13?

- Not available: debtor is not an individual and has very large secured and unsecured debts.       
    

Chapter 7 vs. 11:

- The corporation technically could file Chapter 7 or 11.       
    
- As CEO, you do **not** want liquidation (Chapter 7) because:
    
    - Airline would shut down.       
        
    - You would lose your job; employees lose jobs; reputational harm.       
        

Preferred Chapter 11:

- Encourage board to file Chapter 11:
    
    - You keep your job during reorganization.       
        
    - If you succeed in turning around the company, you may keep a long‑term position or be seen as a hero, improving future career prospects.       
        
    - Even if reorganization ultimately fails, Chapter 11 gives you time to find another job before possible later Chapter 7 liquidation.       
        
- Trustee can:
    
    - Reduce substantial components of total debt.       
        
    - Even terminate existing union contracts and force renegotiation to lower costs.       
        
- If business plan fails, trustee can later convert to Chapter 7 and liquidate.
