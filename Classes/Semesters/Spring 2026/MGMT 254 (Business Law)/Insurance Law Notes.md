##  Big picture: What insurance is and why it exists

- Insurance is **not** gambling from the consumer’s perspective. You cannot come out ahead; at best you break even, and usually you “lose” (you pay premiums and never collect).  (
    
- Economic function: pool risks of many individuals/businesses so a catastrophic loss (fire, major accident, etc.) does not financially wipe out one person. You pay a deductible; insurer covers losses above that up to policy limits.     
    
- Insurance law is basically contract law plus state regulation.
    
    - Usual contract elements: offer, acceptance, consideration (premium), competent parties, lawful purpose, form required by law.     
        
    - “Form required by law”: insurance contracts have to be in writing because the product is intangible.     
        
- States regulate insurance with statutes and case law that define terms, allocate burdens of proof, and control insurer behavior (e.g., bad faith rules).

## 2. Insurable interest

## A. Insurable interest in property

- Core test: Do you suffer a financial loss if the property is damaged/destroyed? If yes, you have an insurable interest.     
    
- Owner of real property (house, commercial building):
    
    - If building burns or is torn away by tornado, owner suffers loss → has insurable interest.     
        
- Tenant (renter):
    
    - No insurable interest in the building itself (real property), because they do not own it.
        
    - Tenant does have insurable interest in personal property inside (furniture, clothing, equipment).     
        
- Exception: Lease requires tenant to buy building insurance and add landlord as “additional insured.”
    
    - Tenant must pay premium; if tenant fails and building burns, tenant will suffer a financial loss because they breached contract; this creates an insurable interest in that building.     
        

## B. Insurable interest in life

- Everyone has an insurable interest in their own life.     
    
- Spouses:
    
    - If one dies, the other often suffers economic loss (income, services around the house) → insurable interest.     
        
- Parents and children:
    
    - Parents can insure children even though kids are currently a net cost; expectation that children will support parents later gives a recognized insurable interest.     
        
- Strangers / unrelated persons: generally no insurable interest.
    
    - Example: professor cannot just insure a random student’s life; no financial loss if student dies.     
        
- Employer “key person” insurance:
    
    - Business can insure certain key employees (e.g., CEO, CFO) because death would cause financial loss to company.     
        
- Workaround idea: person with no insurable interest cannot buy policy on another’s life, but that other person can buy a policy on themselves and name anyone as beneficiary.

## 3. Interpretation of insurance contracts

- Insurance policies are contracts drafted by the insurer.     
    
- If a term is ambiguous (reasonably capable of two meanings) and insured’s interpretation is reasonable, courts will interpret ambiguity **against** the insurer (the drafter) and in favor of the insured.     
    
- To reduce ambiguity, insurers:
    
    - Include pages of definitions at the beginning of the policy.
        
    - If policy definition exists, courts use that.     
        
- If no policy definition, court looks to:
    
    - Statutory definitions.
        
    - Case law defining the term.     
        
- If still unclear, default rule: construe against insurer, in favor of coverage.

## 4. Deductibles and loss allocation

- Deductible: amount insured “absorbs” before insurer pays anything.     
    
- Example (Purdue):
    
    - Purdue paid about 2.18 million in property insurance premiums; had a 250,000 deductible and about 1 billion in coverage.
        
    - If a building sustains minor damage below 250k, insurer pays nothing.     
        
- Same structure applies to personal policies: auto, homeowner’s, etc., with common deductibles like 250, 500, or 1,000.

## 5. Burden of proof in insurance cases

- Civil “burden of proof” = preponderance of the evidence (>50%), not beyond a reasonable doubt.     
    
- Claim by insured (e.g., theft, fire): insured bears burden to prove:
    
    - That a covered loss occurred (e.g., property was stolen, building burned), and
        
    - The extent/value of the loss.     
        
- Example: theft claim
    
    - Friend’s house completely emptied by burglars in Fresno, CA. He claims 53 sweaters. Insurer suspicious (man, desert climate, unusually high sweater count → potential fraud).     
        
    - Insurer looks for proof: receipts, photos, witness testimony. Thieves even took photos and records.
        
    - Claims adjuster unexpectedly hears insured’s coworker (professor’s wife) say he had “at least 100 sweaters.” This corroboration helps meet burden of proof, undercuts fraud suspicion, supports paying the claim.     
        
- If insurer sues for unpaid premiums:
    
    - Insurer is plaintiff; has burden to show premium not paid.

## 6. Bad faith in insurance law

## A. Contrast with ordinary business negotiacions

- In a purely commercial contract (e.g., selling goods for 200,000 on credit):
    
    - Buyer cannot pay full amount, offers 150k; seller might demand 200k.
        
    - Parties negotiate; seller may accept 175k because lawsuit would cost 25–45k and delay payment.
        
    - It is legal (though maybe morally questionable) for buyer to use leverage of litigation costs.     
        

## B. Bad faith in insurance context

- Insurance scenario:
    
    - Policy covers 10,000 in collision damages; deductible is 500 → insurer owes 9,500.  
        
    - Insurer offers only 5,000 and tells insured “sue us” knowing litigation will cost insured 25–45k, more than the disputed 4,500.      
        
- Insurer clearly owes full 9,500 under the contract. Refusing to pay what is owed and leveraging cost of litigation against an individual insured can constitute **bad faith**.      
    
- Bad faith gives insured extra remedies beyond standard breach of contract:
    
    - Contract damages (the 9,500).
        
    - Attorney’s fees (statutory; exception to the American Rule).
        
    - Punitive damages (potentially very high) to punish and deter wrongful insurer conduct.      
        
- Rationale: bargaining power is unequal; society views insurers as large entities and insureds as individuals needing protection, so the law allows punitive remedies to discourage abusive practices.

## 7. Subrogation

- Subrogation: transfer of legal rights to recover a debt or claim from insured to insurer after insurer pays.      
    
- Example:
    
    - You are stopped at a red light; uninsured motorist rear-ends you, causing 10,000 damage.      
        
    - You carry uninsured motorist coverage; your insurer pays you for the loss.
        
    - Your insurer then “steps into your shoes” and sues or pursues the at‑fault uninsured driver (subrogation).      
        
- Practical reality: if negligent party has no assets, insurer might not recover; that is insurer’s risk.      
    
- Subrogation is also used with underinsured motorist coverage and property situations where another party is liable and insurer has paid the insured.

## 8. Homeowners and renters (tenants) insurance

## A. Three main coverages in homeowners policies

Homeowners insurance typically covers three categories:      

1. Liability
    
    - Your legal responsibility for injuries to third parties caused by your negligence.
        
    - Examples: guest trips over your kid’s toy on your property; golfer accidentally hits someone with a golf ball; someone falls into a hole in your yard.      
        
2. Real property (dwelling)
    
    - The house or structure itself; covers damage from perils like fire up to policy limits, less deductible.      
        
3. Personal property
    
    - Your movable property: TVs, cameras, watches, clothing, etc.
        
    - Covered for theft or damage (fire, etc.) usually anywhere in the world, not just at the residence.      
        
    - Example: luggage + laptop stolen in Germany → can claim under homeowner’s insurance, subject to deductible and limits.      
        

- Limits and sub‑limits: separate maximums for high-risk items (jewelry, cash, guns). You can buy endorsements (riders) for higher coverage.      
    

## B. Replacement value vs fair market value

- Traditional policies pay **fair market value**: original cost minus depreciation.
    
    - Example: laptop originally cost 2,000 but is now worth 200 → insurer pays ~200.      
        
- Replacement cost coverage (higher premium) pays what it costs to buy a new equivalent item today.
    
    - Same stolen laptop: insurer pays cost of current comparable model. You don’t have to buy the same item, but that’s the payout benchmark.      
        

## C. Renters (tenants) insurance

- Renters/tenants policies are similar to homeowner’s but exclude real property coverage because renter does not own the building.      
    
- Coverage:
    
    - Personal property (your stuff).
        
    - Liability (your negligence causing injury on premises).

## 9. Business insurance and marine insurance

## A. Business property/liability policies

- Business insurance can mirror homeowner’s categories: liability, real property, and personal property (business personal property).      
    
- Key differences:
    
    - Business can choose any combination: just liability; liability + real property; real property only; or all three.      
        
    - Insurable interest logic:
        
        - Building owner: insures real property and usually liability (premises defects). No insurable interest in tenant’s furniture, computers, etc.      
            
        - Tenant business: insures its own business personal property and liability for injuries on premises; typically no insurable interest in the building itself (unless contract requires).      
            
    - Business personal property coverage is usually location‑specific: covered only at the specified business location.      
        

## B. Inland marine and ocean marine insurance

- Problem: business property that is away from the main location (e.g., laptops, contractor tools, goods in transit) often is **not** covered under standard business property policy.      
    
- Inland marine insurance:
    
    - Covers business personal property away from the fixed business location (on a truck, on a job site, etc.).
        
    - Example: construction company’s tools stolen from truck or job site; or faculty laptop used off campus, covered under an inland marine policy.      
        
- Ocean marine insurance:
    
    - Covers property in transit over oceans (ships, barges, etc.).      
        
- Distinction:
    
    - Inland marine = over land or inland waters (rivers, lakes, planes, trucks, trains, etc.).
        
    - Ocean marine = over the ocean.      
        
- Example nuance: faculty laptop on flight over Atlantic is technically ocean risk but Purdue declines ocean marine coverage because probability (plane crash) is very low and few faculty travel that way; risk not worth premium.

## 10. Automobile insurance

## A. Pricing factors and discrimination

- Premiums depend heavily on:
    
    - Age, gender, driving record, type/age of vehicle, education, occupation, etc.  
        
- Example: yearly cost for older Ford:
    
    - Adult: 548.
        
    - 17‑year‑old girl: 1,040.
        
    - 17‑year‑old boy: 1,907.   
        
- This is discriminatory, but legally allowed because it is **actuarially** based on past accident and claim data by demographic.   
    

## B. Core auto coverages

1. Liability insurance
    
    - Covers your legal responsibility to others (bodily injury and property damage) when you are negligent (e.g., fail to stop at intersection and cause collision).   
        
2. Comprehensive
    
    - Not required by states, but required by lenders if you finance the car.
        
    - Covers non‑collision perils: theft, vandalism, fire, maybe other perils depending on policy.
        
    - Subject to deductible (250, 500, 1,000, etc.).   
        
3. Collision
    
    - Also typically required by lenders.
        
    - Pays to repair your car when damaged in an accident (collision with another car or object), subject to a deductible.   
        
4. Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage
    
    - Protects you if at‑fault driver has **no** insurance.
        
    - Your insurer pays your damages (up to your UM limits) and then subrogates against the uninsured driver.   
        
5. Underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage
    
    - Applies when the at‑fault driver has insurance but **not enough** to cover your loss.
        
    - Example: tortfeasor has 100k liability; your total damages are 250k. Their policy pays 100k; your UIM can pay additional 150k if your limits are at least that.   
        
    - Your insurer then subrogates against the at‑fault driver for that 150k.   
        

## C. Fatal accident data and risk

- Highest fatal accident rate: 16–19‑year‑olds and those 85+. Rates fall as drivers age from late teens into middle age.   
    
- Young drivers: faster reaction time but less experience, more risky behavior → higher accident and claim rates → higher premiums.   
    

## D. Intentional acts vs accidents

- Auto policies cover **accidents**, usually negligence, not intentional harm.   
    
- Example: driver intentionally runs over someone and drives into a building:
    
    - No coverage under auto policy because it is an intentional act, not an “accident.”   
        
- If same physical outcome but due to negligence (too fast for conditions, loses control, crashes into house), liability covers others’ damages and collision covers driver’s car.   
    

## E. State rules for UM/UIM

- Some states (about half) require UM coverage; some require UIM; some allow one, both, or neither.   
    
- Example: Indiana does not require UM/UIM; you can buy either, both, or neither.   
    
- Example: Washington: if you select one, you must have both, due to litigation over what “uninsured” vs “underinsured” means.   
    

## F. More pricing examples

- 20‑year‑old male, two minor speeding tickets, insuring a 2008 Corvette:
    
    - About 1,871 per six months (~3,743/year).   
        
- Older male (professor), same car, clean record:
    
    - About 314.96 per six months (~629.92/year).   
        
- Strategy to reduce cost for young high‑risk driver:
    
    - Choose an older sports car (e.g., 1976 Corvette) — cheaper to repair, less likely stolen.
        
    - Example: 18‑year‑old, two minor tickets, 1976 Corvette: ~891.92 per six months. Same older adult: ~154.64 per six months.

## 11. Life insurance

## A. Types of life insurance

1. Term life insurance
    
    - Pure insurance: you pay premium for a set term (e.g., 20 or 30 years).
        
    - No investment component or cash value; if you die during term, beneficiary gets face amount (e.g., 100k). If you outlive term, no payout and no refund.   
        
    - Cheapest option; most commonly purchased by individuals.   
        
    - Since 1986, employers generally only provide term life if they want a tax deduction (up to 50k per employee in coverage). No deduction for employer‑provided whole life, so businesses largely offer term only.   
        
2. Whole life insurance
    
    - Combines life insurance with an investment/savings component.
        
    - Part of premium goes to pure insurance cost, part is invested to build cash value.   
        
    - If policy runs to its “end” (e.g., age 65/70) or you surrender it, you can receive accumulated cash value.
        
    - If you die during coverage, beneficiary receives only the face amount (e.g., 100k); the “extra” investment value does not go to beneficiary.   
        
3. Endowment policies
    
    - Entire premium (sometimes large up‑front payment) is paid early; policy guarantees a payout at a certain future date or at death, whichever comes first.   
        
    - Attractive for individuals who earn a lot now but expect lower income later (e.g., pro athletes, some actors).
        
    - Not very commonly purchased.   
        

## B. Term vs whole life example

- Suppose you have whole life 100k; friend has term 100k.
    
    - If you die during coverage, your beneficiary gets 100k; friend’s beneficiary also gets 100k.   
        
    - You paid higher premiums; friend paid much less.
        
    - Only scenario where whole life “wins” is if insured lives to end of policy and collects cash value; otherwise, from cost/benefit perspective, term is more efficient for pure risk protection.   
        

## C. Double indemnity

- Double indemnity clause: doubles payout (2× face value) for accidental death, but excludes death by natural causes.   
    
    - Example: 100k face value. If insured dies in a qualifying accident (e.g., car crash), beneficiary gets 200k.   
        
    - No double payout for death from heart attack, stroke, or other natural causes.   
        
- Younger people more likely to die from accidents than natural causes, so double indemnity is more likely to pay for them than for older individuals.   
    
- Exclusions: high‑risk activities such as piloting a private plane, skydiving, suicide, etc., often excluded.   
	

## D. Incontestability clause

- State law requires that after a certain period (e.g., 2 years in Indiana) life insurer cannot contest the policy for misstatements, except for limited exceptions (like outright fraud).   
    
- If the insured made a mistake in the application and survives past the incontestability period, insurer must pay later claim despite that mistake. Insurer is expected to discover application issues during that time frame.

## 12. Workers’ compensation

- Workers’ compensation: state‑mandated insurance system; employers must buy coverage for employees’ work‑related injuries.   
    
- Covered injuries must be “within the scope of employment.”
    
    - Not limited to employer’s premises; offsite work events can be covered if part of job.   
        
- Exclusions:
    
    - Self‑inflicted injuries (e.g., intentionally banging head on wall out of frustration).
        
    - Injuries while employee is under influence of drugs or alcohol.   
        
- Benefits:
    
    - All medical expenses related to the work injury.
        
    - Portion of lost wages (not 100%).
        
    - Short‑term or long‑term disability payments.
        
    - Death benefits (paid to estate or beneficiaries, not to deceased worker).
