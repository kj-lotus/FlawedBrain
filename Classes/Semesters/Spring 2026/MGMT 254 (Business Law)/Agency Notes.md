## Core definition of agency

- **Agency**: A relationship based on express (oral or written) or implied agreement where:
    
    - One person (the **agent**)
        
    - Is authorized to act **under the control** of
        
    - And **for** another (the **principal**)
        
    - By entering into contracts with third parties on the principal’s behalf.
        
- Key elements:
    
    - Agreement: Express (verbal/written) or implied by conduct; it does **not** have to be a full, enforceable contract, just an agreement.  
        
    - Control: The principal must have the right to control the agent’s **means** (day‑to‑day methods) of performing the task.  
        
    - Third parties: The agent’s role is to negotiate and make contracts with third parties for the principal.  
        
- Contrast:
    
    - You can be:
        
        - Employee but **not** an agent (no authority to contract with third parties).
            
        - Agent but **not** an employee (e.g., real estate agent, attorney).
            
        - Both employee **and** agent (e.g., purchasing manager who signs vendor contracts).

## Employee vs. agent vs. independent contractor

## Employee

- Works under the employer’s control over daily activities (hours, tasks, methods).  
    
- May or may not be an agent:
    
    - Employee only: 15‑year‑old ranch hand who has no authority to sell cattle, ranch, cars.  
        
    - Employee and agent: Faculty member using Purdue credit card, entering contracts for travel, syllabus as a contract with students.  
        

## Agent (general concept)

- Person authorized to act under principal’s control and enter into contracts with third parties.  
    
- Examples:
    
    - Insurance agent, real estate agent, attorney handling settlement negotiations.  
        
    - Manager with implied authority to contract within scope of job.  
        

## Independent contractor (IC)

- Definition: One who exercises an **independent employment**, contracts to do a piece of work **according to their own methods**, and is **not subject to control** of the employer except as to the result.  
    
- Principal does **not** control the **means** (day‑to‑day details), only outcome.  
    
- Typically:
    
    - No agency (no authority to contract with third parties), unless facts change.
        
    - No employment relationship for tax and benefit purposes.  
        

## Control over “means” – why it matters

- If the principal starts controlling:
    
    - When the worker shows up and leaves.
        
    - Detailed methods and routes.
        
    - How tasks are done step‑by‑step.
        
- Then the IC may be reclassified as an **employee** and possibly an **agent** (if they also contract with third parties).  
    
- Examples:
    
    - General contractor (IC) for a building: remains an IC unless owner dictates hours, methods, and directs subs in detail, which can create agency.  
        
    - FedEx drivers labeled “independent contractors”:
        
        - Had to be there at specific times, used company equipment, supervised by managers.
            
        - Functionally employees, so FedEx liable for contracts, torts, and employer Social Security payments.

## Liability: contracts, torts, and taxes

## Contract liability

- **Actual agency (express or implied)**:
    
    - There is an agreement between principal and agent.
        
    - If third party knows who the principal is (disclosed principal), **principal is liable** on the contract.
        
    - Agent has **no liability** to third party (assuming within authority).  
        
- Example (manager):
    
    - Manager enters contract with vendor for company; it’s reasonable given manager role.
        
    - Principal (employer) is liable; agent not liable if principal disclosed.  
        

## Tort liability (respondeat superior and vicarious liability)

- If agent is under principal’s control (means), principal can be liable for agent’s negligence.  
    
- Vocabulary:
    
    - **Respondeat superior**: Employer liability for employee’s torts in scope of employment.  
        
    - **Vicarious liability**: Principal’s liability for agent’s acts.  
        
- Arby’s/Corvette example:
    
    - Professor tells you:
        
        - Use his 1976 Corvette.
            
        - Stay under 65 mph at a specific curve.
            
        - Avoid Starbucks driveway, use the second Arby’s driveway to avoid potholes.
            
    - Professor clearly controls **means** (route, speed, specific driveway).  
        
    - You are an **agent**, not an IC; professor is liable on:
        
        - Contract with Arby’s (food purchase).
            
        - Torts (negligence) if you crash while doing the errand.  
            
    - Fatal crash scenario:
        
        - Total damages = 3 million.
            
        - Insurance only covers part; principal can owe remaining 1.7 million personally.  
            
    - If professor did **not** control means, you would be an IC, and he would avoid tort liability.  
        

## Tax and benefits liability

- Employees:
    
    - Employer pays about 7.65% Social Security and Medicare (half the total).  
        
    - Employer usually pays benefits: health, dental, vacation, etc.  
        
- Independent contractors:
    
    - Pay **full** Social Security/Medicare themselves.  
        
    - No employer benefits.
        
    - As a result, an employee can cost 35–50% more than an IC doing the same work at same hourly rate.  
        
- Employers may mislabel workers as “independent contractors” to:
    
    - Avoid payroll taxes and benefits.
        
    - Avoid tort and contract liability.  
        
    - But the law looks at **what actually happens**, not labels or letters (e.g., letters calling the professor “Dear independent contractor”).

## Types of authority: actual and apparent

## Actual authority

- Comes from the actual relationship/understanding between principal and agent:
    
    - **Express**: Oral or written instructions giving specific powers.  
        
    - **Implied**: Authority reasonably necessary to carry out express duties or arising from position (e.g., manager).  
        
- If within actual authority and principal is disclosed:
    
    - Principal is liable.
        
    - Agent not liable to third party.  
        

## Apparent authority

- Occurs when:
    
    - The **principal creates conditions** that make it appear a person is their agent.
        
    - Third party has a **justifiable belief** that the person has authority, even if there is no actual authority.  
        
- Result:
    
    - Principal is liable to third party.
        
    - Principal can seek reimbursement (indemnity) from the “agent” who exceeded authority.  
        
- Purchasing/embezzlement example:
    
    - Purchasing specialist is fired for embezzlement but still contacts vendors and directs them to deliver goods to new location.
        
    - Employer never notified vendors that this person was terminated.
        
    - Vendors reasonably believe ex‑employee is still an agent (apparent authority).
        
    - Employer is liable to vendors; can sue ex‑employee, but recovery is unlikely because goods are fenced and money spent.  
        
- Purdue recruitment/scholarship example:
    
    - Setup: Purdue rents space at a Sheraton, sets up booths and laptops, sends faculty plus staff to meet 17–18‑year‑olds.  
        
    - Faculty member (no actual authority) offers a student:
        
        - Full scholarship: tuition, room, and books, about 40,000 per year for 4 years (160,000 total).  
            
    - Student asks for it in writing; faculty writes a note with date, amount, signs it.
        
    - More than one year in duration, so must be in writing – becomes a legally enforceable contract.  
        
    - No express or implied authority for faculty to grant scholarships:
        
        - Purdue still **liable** because the setup (booth, laptop, faculty presence) creates apparent authority for a 17‑year‑old.  
            
    - Purdue can then collect 160,000 from the faculty member (judgment, wage deductions over many years).

## Disclosed, partially disclosed, and undisclosed principals

- **Disclosed principal**:
    
    - Third party knows the principal’s identity.
        
    - Principal is liable on the contract; agent is not (if within authority).  
        
    - Example: “Purdue University, by Professor X” on a supply contract.
        
- **Partially disclosed principal**:
    
    - Third party knows there is a principal but not who it is (e.g., “I’m contracting for a university”).  
        
    - Agent is personally liable to third party.
        
    - Principal normally must reimburse agent if the agent acted properly; risk if principal goes bankrupt.  
        
- **Undisclosed principal**:
    
    - Third party believes they are dealing only with the agent; no mention of any principal.  
        
    - Agent is liable to third party.
        
    - Principal may still be liable behind the scenes, but third party can sue agent directly.  
        
- Practical advice:
    
    - If signing for a principal/employer, sign in a way that clearly indicates:
        
        - Principal’s name, and
            
        - That you are signing “by” or “for” them (e.g., “Purdue University by [Your Name], [Title]”).

## Who can be an agent, and types of agents

## Who can be an agent?

- Adults with capacity: obvious.  
    
- **Minors**:
    
    - Can be agents.
        
    - If a minor contracts on their own behalf, they can generally disaffirm; but if you (a competent principal) hire a minor as your agent, **you** are bound even if they make mistakes.  
        
- **Corporations**:
    
    - Can serve as agents for others.  
        
- **Aliens (non‑citizens/unauthorized workers)**:
    
    - Can be agents; making them employees is more complex legally, but agency is still possible.  
        
- **Unions**:
    
    - For unionized employees:
        
        - Employees = principals.
            
        - Union = agent.
            
        - Union negotiates with employer on employees’ behalf, and employees vote to accept terms.  
            

## Types of agents

- **Special agent**:
    
    - Authorized to do one or a few specific acts.
        
    - Examples:
        
        - Professor using Purdue card strictly for gas/hotel/airfare.  
            
        - Student picking up food under controlled means for the professor.  
            
        - Power of attorney limited to selling a car or a specific parcel of real estate.  
            
- **General agent**:
    
    - Authorized to conduct a broad range of business of a particular kind.
        
    - Example:
        
        - Person running a corporation’s day‑to‑day operations, entering many types of contracts but not selling corporate real estate or the corporation itself without board approval.  
            
- **Universal agent**:
    
    - Authorized to do **anything** that the principal can legally delegate.
        
    - Usually created by a **universal power of attorney**:
        
        - Must be in writing.
            
        - Signed in front of a notary.
            
        - Can allow spouse, for example, to sell all property, deal with employer, license cars, etc.  
            
    - Limitations (things you cannot delegate):
        
        - Serving in the military in your place.
            
        - Serving a criminal sentence in your place.
            
        - Voting in a political election.

## Independent contractor examples and misclassification

- **Newspaper delivery “independent motor route contractor”**:
    
    - Drives personal car, delivers newspapers at around 5:30 AM.  
        
    - Newspaper:
        
        - Specifies who should receive papers and by what time.
            
        - Does **not** control route, does not let carrier sign up new subscribers (no contracting with third parties).
            
    - Result:
        
        - Carrier is an IC, not an employee and not an agent.
            
        - If carrier causes an accident or breaks a window, newspaper avoids vicarious liability.  
            
- **University “independent contractor” letter**:
    
    - University pays professor to serve on dissertation committee, flies them to New Jersey.  
        
    - Sends letter calling him an “independent contractor” to avoid employee status and tax liability.
        
    - But law looks at actual control and relationship, not labels.  
        
- **Large companies (FedEx, Microsoft, Caterpillar)**:
    
    - Use “contract workers” or “independent contractors” to cut costs (no benefits, no employer Social Security).  
        
    - Attorneys general and IRS may challenge classification using multi‑factor tests (e.g., the “20‑factor” IRS test).
