**1. Deeds and Showing Ownership of Real Property**

**A. Basic concepts**

- Real property = land + anything attached for its reasonable life (buildings, fixtures, etc.).​
- Ownership of **real property** is evidenced by **deeds**; for cars, it’s by **title**.​

**B. Warranty deed vs. quitclaim deed**

**1. Warranty deed**

- Expressly labeled as a “warranty deed” on the document.​
- Grantor (seller) makes **covenants (promises)** about title:

- That they own the estate they purport to convey (usually fee simple).
- That title is free from undisclosed **liens** (e.g., mortgages, judgment liens).
- That no one else has a superior claim to the property.
- That they will **defend** the grantee’s title if someone sues and will pay litigation costs and any resulting damages.​​

- If lawsuit later proves grantor did not have full title, they must compensate grantee for the diminished value and defense costs for the life of the grantor and through distribution of their estate.​

**2. Quitclaim (quick claim) deed**

- Often labeled “quitclaim deed.”​
- Grantor conveys **only whatever interest they currently have**, if any.
- No promise they own fee simple, no promise there are no liens, no promise that someone else won’t show up with a better title.​​
- Equivalent to selling property strictly **“as is”** as to title; grantee takes risk.​
- Common uses:
ASK ABOUT WHY PEOPLE DONT ALWAYS USE WARRANTY DEEDS WHEN BUYING
- Clearing up title problems between family members or co‑owners.
- Selling low‑value property or where grantor is unsure of boundary lines or past use issues.​

**3. Buyer vs. seller preferences**

- **Buyer** prefers **warranty deed** (especially if seller has assets) so they can shift risk and litigation costs back to seller if a title problem appears.​
- **Seller** prefers **quitclaim deed** to cap their risk at closing and avoid long‑term exposure.​

**2. Easements and Prescriptive Easements**

**A. Easements generally**

- **Easement**: non‑possessory right to use land of another for a limited purpose (travel, utilities, support).​​
- Does not transfer ownership; underlying fee remains with landowner.​

**Common easement examples from transcript**

- Streets in front of Krannert/Rawls:

- Adjacent owners may own underlying land to the centerline, but city has easement for **roads and utilities** (water, sewer, electric).​

- Utility easements on recorded plats: right for power/water companies to place and maintain lines.​

**B. Prescriptive easements**

- Created by long, continuous, adverse **use** (not full occupation) of another’s land.​​
- Requirements parallel adverse possession **except** that result is only a **use right**, not ownership:

- Open and notorious use.
- Adverse/hostile (without permission).
- Continuous for statutory period.​​

- Typical uses: walking or **driving** across someone’s land to access a road/house; utility lines crossing outside of recorded easement.​

**Power‑line example (Idaho garage roof)**

- Professor’s planned garage roof peak too close to power lines.​
- Checking recorded easement reveals pole/lines are actually **off** the described easement and trespassing over his land.​
- However, poles/lines had been there for decades → power company likely has a **prescriptive easement** to keep them there.​
- Power company offers to move lines only if professor pays thousands; he declines and redesigns roof to avoid them.​

**Government road built in wrong place**

- County road physically located partially on private land (not exactly where plat shows).​
- After long, open public use, county has acquired a **prescriptive easement** for the actual road location and for associated utilities.​​
- As a general rule, you **cannot obtain adverse possession against government** land; the asymmetry favors the public entity.​​

**3. Adverse Possession (Acquiring Ownership)**

**A. Concept and policy**

- **Adverse possession**: method of acquiring **title** to real property by long‑term, adverse occupancy that effectively “steals” the land in the eyes of the law.​​
- Justification:

- Land is long‑lasting (unlike phones, laptops).
- Legal system wants land to be used, not left idle.
- Encourages owners to monitor and protect their property.​​

**B. Typical elements (state‑by‑state wording varies)**

1. **Actual** possession – claimant physically uses land (builds structures, farms, lives there, fences it).​​
2. **Open and notorious** – visible occupancy so that a reasonably attentive owner would notice.​
3. **Hostile (adverse)** – without owner’s permission; owner can treat claimant as trespasser.​​
4. **Exclusive** – claimant uses land without sharing possession with true owner.​
5. **Continuous** – uninterrupted for the full statutory period, as ordinary use of that type of land would be (e.g., seasonal farming may count as continuous).​​
6. **Statutory period** – varies by state (10 years in Indiana, 5 in Idaho with taxes, 10 in Washington).​​

Some states add:

- **Color of title**: claimant holds a defective deed or other document that appears to convey title.​​
- **Payment of property taxes** on the disputed land.​​

**C. Personal property vs. real property**

- **Personal property**: thief never acquires title by adverse possession; true owner can reclaim from thief or any downstream buyer.​​
- **Real property**: adverse possessor can acquire full title after statutory period; true owner loses it.​​

**D. Classic “Old West” example**

- Rancher owns thousands of acres; farmer moves in, fences a small area, builds log house, grazes cattle/goats, plants crops.​
- After continuous, open, hostile use for statutory period (e.g., 10 years in Indiana), farmer acquires title to fenced tract by adverse possession.​

**E. Mowing grass vs. farming**

- Suburban example:

- Original owner mows right to property line.
- Next owner “over‑mows” 5 feet into neighbor’s yard because neighbor lets grass grow too long.​

- In Indiana, mowing is not year‑round; winter breaks continuity → no adverse possession.​
- But where land is used for **farming**, seasonal plowing and planting over boundary can count as continuous.​​

- To avoid accidental adverse possession, farmers often place **rock piles or fences** on true property lines.​

**F. Fence and “tacking” example (cul‑de‑sac)**

- Third owner builds privacy fence where grass has been mowed, unknowingly placing fence 5 feet into neighbor’s lot.​
- Neighbor never objects; both think fence is on boundary.
- After 10 years in Indiana, the fenced 5‑foot strip can become owner’s property by adverse possession.​
- **Tacking**: successive possessors can add their adverse possession periods if there is privity (voluntary transfer):

- First owner has fence encroachment for 4 years, second for 6 more → 10 total; second owner can perfect adverse title.​​

**G. Washington 160‑acre family land example**

- Professor’s parents own 160 acres (80 each side of road).​
- Behind their land, a rancher removes old fence and rebuilds fence 15 feet inside parents’ land, effectively taking a strip.​
- As eighth grader, professor tells father; father doesn’t mind because new fence is totally on rancher’s side → father avoids paying half fence cost (equity rule for boundary fences).​
- Under Washington law, to get adverse possession in shortened period, claimant must have **color of title** and pay **taxes** on disputed strip; rancher never paid those taxes (parents did).​
- Result: rancher likely cannot perfect adverse title under Washington’s tax requirement, even though fence is there for years.​​

**H. Idaho lake property A‑frame example – defeating adverse possession**

Facts:​

- Professor buys an acre off lake with two narrow 15‑foot strips down to water.
- Years later, finds an A‑frame structure maybe on his parcel.
- He checks plat at county, then paces off distances – boundaries still uncertain.
- Talks to older man sitting by A‑frame, who says:

- He built small house 2 years ago.
- He lives there only in summer; lives in Spokane rest of year.
- House was originally 100 feet away on his own lot; high water floated it to current spot because it sits on styrofoam from old docks.

Legal impact:

- **Continuity**: seasonal use only (summer) – not year‑round continuous possession.​
- **Time**: only 2 years, shorter than Idaho’s 5‑year requirement.​​
- **Movement**: structure physically moved to new location last year; new adverse possession clock would start then.​
- **Taxes**: Idaho requires adverse possessor to pay **property taxes**; neighbor isn’t paying taxes on this parcel.​​

Conclusion: neighbor cannot claim professor’s land by adverse possession; professor wisely avoids expensive survey.​

**I. Driving and paving over neighbor’s land**

- If neighbor **drives over** part of your land for years to reach garage, that can mature into a **prescriptive easement** (right to keep using route), but not ownership.​
- If neighbor lays **asphalt/concrete** driveway partially on your land and meets statutory period (e.g., 10 years in Indiana), they can obtain **ownership** of the paved strip via adverse possession.​

**J. Squatter in old farmhouse**

- Large corporate farmer stops using farmhouse; squatter moves in without permission, paying no rent.​
- If squatter stays long enough (e.g., 10 years Indiana), openly and exclusively, they can gain title by adverse possession (subject to any state tax/recording requirements).​​

**4. Subdivision, Easements, and Quitclaim Strategy – Spokane 75‑acre Example**

**A. Purchase and valuation**

- Professor buys 75 acres just outside Spokane: 70 contiguous acres + 5 acres across road.​
- Price ≈ price of typical 10‑acre “ranchette,” because buyers mainly want space and privacy, not large acreages.​

**B. Zoning and parceling**

- County zoning: minimum parcel size 10 acres (you cannot legally subdivide into smaller lots).​
- 75 acres → potential for **7 parcels**: six 10‑acre lots + one 15‑acre lot.​
- He sketches layout (A–G, plus H across road).​

**C. Access problem and easements**

- County road runs along front, but only touches four frontage parcels; back parcels A, B, C have no direct road frontage.​
- For those parcels to be legally marketable, he must create **access easements** over his own land.​​
- He designates a 65‑foot wide strip across parcels E and F as an easement (“EASM” on map) to let A, B, C owners enter and exit.​
- Easement is recorded in all relevant deeds to ensure it **runs with the land**.​

**D. Cheap paper plat vs. expensive survey**

- Full survey quote ≈ $10–12k; county instead allows subdivision on paper via legal descriptions for ≈ $600.​
- Because boundaries are not surveyed precisely, he does **not** want long‑term liability for exact metes and bounds.​

**E. Use of quitclaim deeds**

- Sells each parcel by **quitclaim deed**: “I transfer whatever interest I have,” with no promise of perfect boundaries.​
- If later a boundary dispute or adverse possession issue arises between buyer and neighbor, buyer **cannot** force professor to pay litigation costs or compensate them under deed warranties.​

**F. 5‑acre H parcel and angry rancher**

- 5‑acre H parcel across road is combined with G via deed → new owner of G effectively holds both G and H (15 acres).​
- New owners cut barbed‑wire fence along road and put in a gate to use H for horse pasture.​
- Back rancher with thousands of acres arrives, cuts chain, threatens them physically, and claims H is his because it’s been fenced/grazed by his cattle.​

Legal analysis:

- In **Indiana**, long‑term fencing and grazing could equal adverse possession; rancher might own it.​
- In **Washington**, statutory adverse possession requires **taxes** on the disputed strip to be paid by claimant. Rancher never paid taxes on H; professor and prior owner did.​​
- Thus rancher lacks key element, and buyers’ title stands.
- Because professor sold by **quitclaim**, if litigation arose he would not owe defense costs or damages; under a **warranty deed**, he would.​

**G. Parcel D threatened fence move**

- Same rancher tells D‑parcel buyer he will move fence further into D, claiming historic travel route for family’s wagons/cars to reach remote acreage.​
- Old faint ruts and decaying fence show past use; historically, may have been a prescriptive easement at best.​
- But active use stopped years ago; easement, if any, has likely been **abandoned**; no new adverse possession of that portion started.​
- If D owner does nothing and rancher moves fence and then meets Washington’s statutory tax + time requirements, rancher could later claim that strip by adverse possession.​​

**5. Fee Simple vs. Life Estate**

**A. Fee simple (fee simple absolute)**

- Greatest possible interest: potentially infinite duration, full right to possess, use, exclude, and transfer.​​
- Default estate conveyed if deed is silent on duration.​

**B. Life estate**

- Ownership measured by a human life (usually the life tenant’s).​​
- Life tenant’s rights:

- Possess and use property, and take ordinary income (rents, crops).
- Cannot commit **waste** (actions that significantly diminish property’s value for remaindermen).​

- Upon life tenant’s death, property automatically passes to **remaindermen** or reverts to grantor.​

**Remarriage / stepfather example**

- Older first husband dies; younger widow remarries a man similar in age to her adult children.​
- She wants:

- New husband secure in having a home until he dies.
- Adult children to **eventually** get the house, not risk it being left by husband to someone else.​

- Solution: create **life estate** for second husband with remainder to children:

- Husband can live there for life (or rent it out), but can’t leave it by will or sell fee simple.
- At his death, title vests in children automatically.​​

**6. Premises Liability: Trespassers, Licensees, Invitees**

**A. Trespasser**n

- Enters/occupies land without permission or legal right.​
- Duty owed:

- Generally, no duty to make land safe or warn of dangers.
- Must not **intentionally or recklessly** injure trespasser (e.g., no trap guns/shotguns on trip‑wires).​​

- Children may be treated differently under attractive nuisance doctrine (not heavily discussed in this transcript).​

**B. Licensee**

- Has legal right to enter but is not invited for mutual economic benefit.​
- Examples:

- **Mail carrier** (USPS).
- Utility meter reader.
- Police/firefighters responding because someone else called 911.​

- Duty: warn of or correct **known** hidden dangers; no duty to inspect for unknown hazards.​​

**Siena squirrel‑hole example**

- Professor teaching in Siena; ground squirrels create holes outside while he is in class.​
- Mail carrier steps into hole and twists ankle.​
- Mail carrier is **licensee**; professor had no actual knowledge of hole → no liability.​

**C. Invitee**

- On land by express/implied invitation for mutual benefit (business or equivalent).​​
- Examples:

- Store customers.
- FedEx/UPS delivering package you ordered.
- Police/firefighters when **you** call 911.​

- Duty: reasonable care to keep premises reasonably safe; must correct or warn of **known AND reasonably discoverable** hazards (inspection duty).​​

**7. Nuisance (Briefly)**

- **Private nuisance**: substantial, unreasonable interference with use/enjoyment of someone’s land (e.g., odors, noise, runoff).​​
- **Public nuisance**: interference with rights of the community at large (e.g., huge landfill smell, blocked public highway).​​
- Remedies: damages, injunction, or both; public nuisance typically prosecuted by government entity.​​

**8. Eminent Domain**

- Government’s constitutional power to **take private real property** for public use, upon paying **just compensation** (usually fair market value).​​
- Common uses: highways, schools, utilities, parks; also controversial economic development projects.​​
- If owner refuses to sell:

- Government files condemnation action.
- Court determines public use and just compensation via appraisals.
- Upon payment, title transfers by court order/deed.​

**9. Liens on Real Property**

- **Lien**: non‑possessory security interest in property to secure a debt; clouds title.​​

Major lien types mentioned or implied:

- **Tax lien** – unpaid property taxes; after statutory period, government can foreclose and sell property.​
- **Mortgage** – consensual lien securing home loan; if borrower defaults, lender forecloses. (More fully in mortgage chapter but referenced here.)​
- **Judgment lien** – from lawsuit judgment (e.g., auto accident liability above insurance); recorded against debtor’s house and must be satisfied at sale/refinance.​​
- **Mechanic’s/Construction lien** – contractors or tradespeople who improve land and aren’t paid can record lien and may foreclose.​
