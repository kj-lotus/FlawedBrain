## 1. Types of Employment: At‑Will vs. Contract

## Employment at will

- About 90%+ of American employees are **at‑will** employees.​
    
- Core rule:
    
    - Employer can fire you for **any reason or no reason**, as long as it is not an **illegal** reason (e.g., race discrimination).​
        
    - Employee can quit for any reason at any time.​
        
- 49 of 50 states recognize the employment‑at‑will doctrine; **Montana** is the only state that does not.​
    
- In Montana, the employer must have **just cause** to terminate an employee.​
    

## Contract (time‑certain) employment

- A contractual employee works for a **specified period of time** under a written (or sometimes clearly defined verbal) contract.​
    
- If the **employer** terminates the employee before the end of the contract **without cause**, the employer breaches the contract; the employee is entitled to **damages** (lost wages, etc.).​
    
- If the **employee** quits before the contract ends, the employee breaches the contract and the employer may recover **damages** such as: higher cost of a replacement, recruiting costs, training costs, etc.​

## 2. Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)

The FLSA is the main federal statute regulating basic wage and hour rules.​

## Core topics covered by the FLSA

- **Overtime pay** (time‑and‑a‑half) for non‑exempt workers.​
    
- **Minimum wage** requirements.​
    
- **Child labor** restrictions.​
    

## Historical background and the 40‑hour workweek

- Before the 1930s, many production workers typically worked **6 days/week, 10 hours/day** (60 hours per week).​
    
- During the early 1930s and the Great Depression, unemployment peaked in 1933; government estimates suggested about **one‑third of the labor force was unemployed**.​
    
- Original **proposal** for the FLSA considered a **30‑hour** workweek (instead of 40).​
    
- Reasoning:
    
    - If typical work was 60 hours/week, cutting hours would **spread work** to more people.​
        
    - Since about **1/3** of workers were unemployed, they needed to free up **1/3 of 60 hours = 20 hours**, leaving **40 hours** for each worker (60 − 20 = 40).​
        
    - If half the workforce had been unemployed, they might have needed a 30‑hour week; that’s why legislative history suggests we would have a 30‑hour week today if unemployment had been 50% instead of one‑third.​
        
- Overtime rule: For **non‑exempt (hourly) employees**, any hours worked **over 40 in a week** must be paid at **1.5 times** the regular rate.​
    
- **Exempt employees** (salaried managers, supervisors, professionals, many salespeople, some computer professionals, people with access to confidential information) are not entitled to overtime pay; employers can require them to work 50–70+ hours without time‑and‑a‑half.​
    

## Minimum wage and sub‑minimum wage

- Federal minimum wage is **7.25 dollars/hour** (baseline under FLSA).​
    
- States may set a **higher** minimum wage than the federal level; they cannot set a **lower** one (except via special categories like tips).​
    
    - Example: some states such as Maryland and West Virginia have higher state minimums than 7.25 dollars.​
        
- **Sub‑minimum wage** categories:
    
    - Young workers above child‑labor minimum ages can sometimes be paid less under specific exemptions.​
        
    - **Tipped employees** can be paid as low as **2.13 dollars/hour**, as long as **tips plus cash wage** bring their total pay up to at least **7.25 dollars/hour** over the workweek.​
        
    - Examples of tipped employees: restaurant servers, golf course workers handling bags, airport luggage handlers, etc.​
        

## Example: Tipped employee shortfall

- Suppose a restaurant pays servers **2.13 dollars/hour**.​
    
- If one server’s tips are low, and at the end of a 40‑hour week their total (2.13 dollars × 40 + tips) is less than 7.25 dollars/hour on average, the employer **must pay the difference** to bring them to 7.25 dollars.​
    
- However, because most employees do earn enough tips, a server who asks for make‑up pay may be viewed as either:
    
    - A poor server (not worth keeping), or
        
    - Someone trying to cheat.​
        
- Since most servers are **at‑will**, the employer can legally fire them after paying the required shortfall.​
    
- This illustrates how a law designed to **protect** workers can unintentionally lead to someone being fired.​
    

## Breaks and the “20‑minute rule”

- Employers (outside certain states like California) generally are **not required** by federal law to provide rest or meal breaks at all.​
    
- However, many employers provide:
    
    - 15‑minute paid break in the morning
        
    - 15‑minute paid break in the afternoon
        
    - 30‑minute unpaid lunch break​
        
- Under the FLSA:
    
    - Any break **20 minutes or less** must be treated as **paid time**—you **cannot deduct** that time from the employee’s pay.​
        
    - Breaks **over 20 minutes** (like a 30‑minute lunch) **need not be paid**.​
        
- Employers can **discipline** employees for taking unauthorized breaks, but still cannot deduct short (≤20 minutes) breaks from pay.​
    
- If an employer improperly deducts short breaks, employees can seek back pay for up to **two years**.​
    

## Comp time for hourly employees

- “Comp time” = giving time off later instead of paying overtime now.​
    
- Example:
    
    - Hourly employee works **50 hours** Mon–Wed for a project.​
        
    - Employee then works 8 hours Thu and 8 hours Fri (total **66 hours** for the week, with 26 beyond the project days in the example used).​
        
    - Employer offers to “bank” extra hours as comp time instead of overtime pay.​
        
- Under FLSA, for **private‑sector hourly employees**, this is **illegal**:
    
    - The employer MUST pay overtime (1.5×) for any hours over 40 in the week, even if they also give time off later.​
        
    - In the example, the employee would be owed overtime pay on those overtime hours (e.g., 10 extra dollars/hour × 26 hours = 260 dollars if base pay was 20 dollars/hour).​
        
- Comp time is fine for **exempt** (salaried) supervisors and managers, who are not entitled to statutory overtime in the first place.​
    

## Treating exempt workers like hourly employees

- If an employer tells an exempt manager to **“stay late and make up”** a few hours missed that day, they are treating them like a non‑exempt hourly worker.​
    
- If a court finds that an exempt employee was effectively treated as non‑exempt, the employee may be entitled to **back overtime pay** for all weeks in the previous **two years** where they worked more than 40 hours.​
    
- Employers may dock pay for full days absent, but should not dock for small partial‑day absences for exempt employees.​

## 3. National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) and Unions

## Unionization rates and job security

- Around **11%** of the labor force is unionized overall; unionized workers are typically **contractual** rather than at‑will.​
    
- Private‑sector unionization is below **7%**, while government/public‑sector unionization is about **37%**.​
    
- Public employment often has more job security (through unions), even though public agencies often face less competition than private businesses.​
    

## Basic union benefits and employer concerns

- Unions aim to improve:
    
    - Wages
        
    - Hours
        
    - Working conditions
        
    - Job security​
        
- Downsides from management’s viewpoint:
    
    - Reduced **flexibility** in making changes.​
        
    - Higher labor costs; cost of goods/services tends to rise by a bit over **14%** on average in unionized environments, making firms less competitive unless competitors face similar costs.​
        

## Unfair labor practices and back pay

- Under the NLRA, if an employer takes action that violates workers’ organizing and collective rights, it is an **unfair labor practice**.​
    
- Employees harmed by unfair labor practices can obtain:
    
    - **Back pay**, and
        
    - **Reinstatement** (their job back).​
        

## Coalition/“concerted activity” protections

- The NLRA protects not only union members but also **non‑union workers** engaged in **concerted activity**—two or more employees acting together about wages or working conditions.​
    
- Example:
    
    - One employee asking for a raise alone can legally be fired (no NLRA protection for single, individual bargaining).​
        
    - If two workers coordinate and one asks for a raise on behalf of both of them, that is **concerted activity**; firing the spokesperson would be an unfair labor practice.​
        
- Thus:
    
    - **Individual** request about pay/conditions → no NLRA protection.​
        
    - **Group** request (two or more) about pay/conditions → NLRA protection similar to union members.​
        

## Employee speech about pay and conditions

- Managers and supervisors are considered **representatives of the employer**, not “employees” for NLRA purposes, so they do **not** have NLRA protections.​
    
- An employer can legitimately tell a **manager** not to discuss their salary, and can fire them for doing so (no NLRA unfair labor practice).​
    
- **Non‑managerial employees** covered by the NLRA **cannot be prohibited** from discussing:
    
    - Wages
        
    - Benefits
        
    - Working conditions​
        
- A rule forbidding rank‑and‑file employees from talking about their pay would be an **unfair labor practice**, because it prevents them from effectively organizing and bargaining.​
    

## Non‑solicitation policies

- Many employers adopt **no‑solicitation** policies:
    
    - Ban on selling or promoting goods/services on company property (e.g., no Girl Scout cookie sales, no fundraising ticket sales, etc.).​
        
- If a company has a no‑solicitation policy but **routinely allows** casual solicitations (cookies, tickets, etc.), it cannot then selectively ban **union‑related solicitation**.​
    
- To legally enforce a no‑solicitation policy against union organizing, the employer must **enforce it consistently** against all types of solicitation.​
    

## Centralization vs. decentralization and union organizing

- **Centralized** organization:
    
    - Key decisions (hiring, firing, raises, benefits) are made at headquarters.​
        
- **Decentralized** organization:
    
    - Local managers at each location make independent decisions on hiring/firing and raises.​
        
- Impact on unionization:
    
    - In a centralized company with 10,000 employees across 10 sites, unions may need **5,001** pro‑union votes to cover the whole organization.​
        
    - In a decentralized company, a union can organize **one site at a time**; at a 1,000‑employee location, only **501** pro‑union votes might be needed to unionize that location.​
        
    - Success at one location can spread (domino effect).​
        
- Some firms prefer **centralization** partly to make union organizing more difficult.​
    

## Right‑to‑work and the free‑rider problem

- In **right‑to‑work** states:
    
    - Employers and unions **cannot** agree to contracts requiring employees to **join the union** or be fired.​
        
    - About **half** of U.S. states are right‑to‑work states.​
        
- In non‑right‑to‑work states (before change in this state), unions could negotiate **union‑security clauses** requiring membership as a condition of employment.​
    
- In right‑to‑work states, unions must represent **all employees** in the bargaining unit (including non‑members) in grievances and bargaining.​
    
- This creates a **free‑rider problem**:
    
    - Non‑members get the benefits (grievance representation, strike support, better wages/conditions) without paying dues.​
        
    - Over time, membership and union resources may fall, making it harder to sustain the union.​
        
    - Social effect: fewer and weaker unions.​

## 4. Retirement & Social Insurance: ERISA, Social Security, Unemployment

## ERISA and vesting

- **ERISA** (Employee Retirement Income Security Act) governs:
    
    - Pension plans
        
    - 401(k)s
        
    - IRAs
        
    - Many employer‑sponsored retirement and health plans in the private sector.​
        
- Problem before ERISA:
    
    - Employees could work 40+ years and be **fired just before retirement** age so that the employer would not have to pay benefits.​
        
- **Vesting** = the point at which an employee gains a **non‑forfeitable right** to employer‑funded benefits, even if they later quit or are fired.​
    
- Plans typically require a minimum period of service (e.g., 3–10 years) before vesting, depending on the plan type and employer.​
    
- Once vested, the employee keeps rights to their pension/retirement benefits at the appropriate retirement age (e.g., 55, 59½, 62, 65, etc.), regardless of later job changes.​
    

## Social Security and Medicare

- Social Security is a federal system that provides:
    
    - Retirement income (not intended as a full retirement; more like a **minimum floor**, similar to minimum wage).​
        
    - Disability benefits for workers who became disabled after working the required **40 quarters (10 years)**, not necessarily consecutive.​
        
    - Survivor benefits for spouses and children of deceased, vested workers.​
        
    - A small lump‑sum death benefit (about a few hundred dollars).​
        
- **Medicare** provides government health insurance starting at age **65**, but it does not cover all medical costs.​
    

## Unemployment insurance

- Unemployment insurance is a **joint federal‑state program**.​
    
- Standard benefit period is about **6 months**, though extended periods (e.g., up to 18 months) have been offered in severe recessions.​
    
- To qualify, a person must:
    
    - Have a sufficient work history (worked previously).​
        
    - Be terminated **without fault** (e.g., layoff);
        
    - Not have been fired for misconduct such as theft or chronic tardiness, and
        
    - Not have voluntarily quit, unless there was a **major change** in working conditions.​
        

## Examples of “significant change in conditions”

- If a professor at Krannert is reassigned to teach **physics** in a different college, that is a major change; quitting would still allow unemployment benefits.​
    
- Changing an employee from a normal day shift to a midnight shift, knowing they likely cannot accommodate it due to family obligations, may count as a major change; if they quit, they may still qualify for benefits.​
    

## Going to school full‑time

- Generally, **full‑time students** (e.g., 12+ credit hours at Purdue) do **not** qualify for unemployment benefits, because the assumption is they are not available for full‑time work.​
    
- There may be narrow exceptions if someone is both working full time and enrolled in full‑time classes, but this is unusual.​
    

## Amount of unemployment benefits

- In Indiana, typical maximum weekly benefit is about **390‑plus dollars**, with a maximum total around **10,000 dollars** over six months.​
    
- Someone making 100,000 dollars/year would normally earn about **50,000 dollars** in six months; unemployment would provide only about **10,000 dollars** for that period.​
    
- Because benefits are much lower than prior wages, and **taxable**, most people are strongly motivated to find new employment quickly.​

## 5. Workplace Safety, Workers’ Comp, and Employee Privacy

## OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration)

- OSHA regulations can be enforced either by the **federal** government or by a **state** plan; Indiana uses a state plan (IOSHA).​
    
- OSHA’s purpose is **preventive** safety regulation:
    
    - Requires employers to provide a **safe work environment**.​
        
    - Issues regulations and standards to reduce workplace hazards.​
        
- Employers can be fined:
    
    - After accidents (e.g., state fair stage collapse, Notre Dame practice filming tower collapse).​
        
    - For unsafe conditions discovered **before** any accident occurs.​
        

## Workers’ compensation

- Workers’ compensation is a **state** system focused on **after‑the‑fact** compensation.​
    
- It typically covers:
    
    - Medical expenses for work‑related injuries or illnesses.​
        
    - Payments for disability (partial or total, temporary or permanent).​
        
    - Benefits for death or loss of life.​
        
- OSHA = prevent accidents and enforce safety standards;  
    Workers’ comp = pay for injuries and losses after accidents occur.​
    

## Employee privacy and employer monitoring

- Private businesses and nonprofits **do not** have constitutional privacy rights or obligations in the same way government does; employees’ privacy expectations come mainly from **contract and policy**, not the Fourth Amendment.​
    
- An employee has protection only where there is a **reasonable expectation of privacy**.​
    

## Eliminating reasonable expectation of privacy

- Employers can eliminate privacy expectations by:
    
    - Written **policies** (employee handbooks, contracts).
        
    - Emails or notices explicitly stating monitoring practices.
        
    - Verbal statements (harder to prove, but still possible).​
        
- Common policies:
    
    - Right to inspect employee desks, lockers, or company‑owned devices.​
        
    - Right to monitor **email** and computer usage for keywords (e.g., trade secrets, negative comments, etc.).​
        
    - Right to search briefcases or bags entering/leaving the premises.​
        
    - Right to use surveillance cameras (camcorders) in the workplace.​
        
- Medium and large firms often use software that scans employee emails for sensitive keywords.​
    
- If employees are **informed** of these practices, there is **no reasonable expectation of privacy**, so the employer’s monitoring is generally lawful.​
    

## Contractual waiver of safety/privacy

- By contract, employees can give up many **safety and privacy** protections in the private sector, as long as it does not violate specific statutes.​
    
- Public employees (government workers) have more **constitutional** protections than private‑sector employees, particularly regarding searches and privacy.
