Tort law = civil wrongs where a plaintiff sues for money damages for injury to person or property (private, civil law, not criminal).​

---

**Core Tort Concepts**

- Tort: Civil wrong (act or omission) causing injury to person, property, or economic interests.​
- Burden of proof: On the plaintiff, by preponderance of the evidence (more than 50%).​
- Act vs omission:

- Act: Doing something that causes harm.
- Omission: Failing to do something required by law (e.g., not clearing snow).​

- Elements of negligence:

1.                  Duty (legal obligation to act with reasonable care).

2.                  Breach of duty.

3.                  Proximate cause (causal connection between breach and harm).

4.                  Damages (actual loss/injury).​

Example: Purdue fails to clear snow from sidewalks within a reasonable time; student (invitee) slips and is injured → duty to keep premises reasonably safe, breach (late or inadequate snow removal), proximate cause (slip due to snow/ice), damages (injuries).​

---

**Negligence & Slip-and-Fall**

- Negligence is the most litigated tort (car accidents, slip‑and‑fall, etc.).​
- Reasonable time: Usually a fact question for judge/jury, but can be fixed by statute/ordinance.​
- West Lafayette snow ordinance:

- Businesses, government, owners/renters must clear sidewalks within 6 hours after snow stops.​
- After 6 hours = automatic breach by law; judge/jury can still find an even shorter “reasonable time.”​

- Invitees vs trespassers:

- Invitees (e.g., students on campus, customers in store): highest duty of care.​
- Trespassers: much lower duty; often no liability for slip‑and‑fall.​

Example twist: Purdue uses an automated system that polishes ice (removes snow but leaves slick ice) → action itself can be a breach if it makes conditions more dangerous (negligent act, not just omission).​

---

**Contributory vs Comparative Negligence**

- Contributory negligence:

- Old rule: If plaintiff is any percentage at fault → no recovery (complete bar).​
- Now used in only a few states plus D.C.​

- Comparative negligence:

- Pure comparative:

- Plaintiff’s recovery reduced by their % of fault; no 50% cutoff.​
- Example: You 40% at fault, damages 10,000 → you recover 6,000 (from other party 60% at fault); they can also recover 4,000 from you if you caused 40% of their losses; netting can occur.​

- 50% rule (modified comparative – use 50% for class):

- If plaintiff is more than 50% at fault → no recovery.​
- Indiana follows 50% rule.​

- Spectrum: Contributory (harsh to plaintiffs) → 50% rule → pure comparative (most pro‑plaintiff in shared‑fault situations).​

Example (Indiana 50% rule): You 40% at fault, other driver 60%, your damages 10,000 → you get 6,000; other driver gets 0 because they’re more than 50% at fault.​

---

**Joint and Several Liability & Deep Pockets**

- Joint and several liability:

- When multiple defendants are liable for the same harm, each is individually liable for the full amount; plaintiff can collect all damages from any one of them.​
- Defendant who overpays can try to seek contribution from others, but often can’t collect (e.g., from an incarcerated mugger).​

- “Deep pocket”:

- The defendant with money/insurance (e.g., hotel, big employer, large business) often ends up paying most or all of the judgment when others can’t.​

Example: Mugger and hotel each 50% at fault for 4 million judgment; mugger has no assets, so hotel ends up paying all 4 million.​

**Liquor Liability & Indiana Furnishing Statute**

- Liquor legal liability: Bars or sellers can buy special insurance for serving alcohol to intoxicated persons.​
- Indiana statute on furnishing alcohol:

- “Furnish” includes barter, deliver, sell, exchange, provide, give away (anything except theft).​
- Default rule: Person who furnishes alcohol is not liable in civil action for injuries caused by the drinker’s intoxication unless:

1. Furnisher had actual knowledge the person was visibly intoxicated when served; and
2. The intoxication was a proximate cause of the death, injury, or damage.​

- Applies to bartenders, bar owners, and private parties (e.g., you bring a six-pack, watch an obviously drunk person take your beer → potential liability).​

- Joint and several: Drunk driver, bartender, and bar can all be jointly and severally liable.​

Only real “escape” from being a furnisher is to treat it as theft (e.g., call police on someone stealing alcohol), which almost no one actually does.​

**Attractive Nuisance & Strict Liability**

- Attractive nuisance:

- Protects minors (traditionally small children) drawn to dangerous conditions (classic example: water on property).​
- Property owner/lessee is liable for child injuries without needing to prove breach as in normal negligence (water, and in Indiana, also large equipment, electrical equipment, etc.).​

- Strict liability is built on attractive nuisance ideas and includes:

- Absolute liability.
- Product liability.​

- Product liability:

- Applies to inherently dangerous products (e.g., trampoline); juries routinely say trampolines are inherently dangerous.​
- Non‑inherently dangerous products (e.g., bottled water) → must sue under negligence instead.​

- Absolute liability (ultra‑hazardous activities):

- Examples: Using dynamite, transporting large amounts of gasoline, keeping wild animals (lions, tigers, bears, deer kept as pets, etc.).​
- Owner is liable for injuries caused by non‑domesticated animals regardless of fault; domesticated animals (cats, dogs, horses, cows, goats) are usually negligence‑based.​

Example: Owner walking a wild cat on a leash in town is absolutely liable if it injures someone, even if declawed; if a bystander kicks the cat to protect children and gets bitten, the bystander’s own intentional act can reduce recovery via comparative negligence concepts.​

**Res Ipsa Loquitur (“The Act Speaks for Itself”)**

- Purpose: Shifts burden of proof from plaintiff to defendant when the accident itself strongly implies negligence and plaintiff cannot know exactly what happened.​
- Requirements (conceptually):

- Injury of a kind that ordinarily does not occur without negligence.
- Instrumentality was under control of defendant.
- Plaintiff did not contribute to harm (in the classic formulation).​

- Effect: Defendant must prove they were not negligent, instead of plaintiff proving they were.​

Examples:

- Surgical sponge left inside patient:

- Patient was unconscious, can’t prove exact negligent act; X‑ray shows sponge with required radio‑tag from hospital; court allows res ipsa loquitur, shifting burden to hospital/surgeon/nurses.​

- Airplane explodes over ocean, cause unknown despite federal investigation:

- Families use res ipsa loquitur, arguing planes don’t just blow up absent negligence or product defect; airline, manufacturer, airport, security contractors can all end up joint and severally liable in shares determined by jury.​

**Immunity & Partial Immunity (Government)**

- Immunity in civil law: Defendant is not liable for damages, so case may be dismissed without trial.​
- Historical origin: “The king can do no wrong” → government immunity in English law → adopted in U.S.; many immunities now reduced or eliminated.​
- Indiana partial governmental immunity:

- Maximum total liability for one incident: 5 million for all victims combined.​
- Maximum per person: 700,000.​

- Effect: Government is partially protected even if clearly negligent.

Example: Lucas Oil Stadium hypothetical:

- City is warned roof may collapse if more than 3 inches of snow accumulate, but delays 12 million repairs; roof collapses with 50,000 people inside, killing everyone.​
- Under Indiana caps: total payout 5 million ÷ 50,000 people = 100 per life, even though real value of life is far greater.​

---

**No-Fault Liability (Workers’ Comp & Some Auto)**

- No-fault concept: Liability doesn’t depend on proving someone else was at fault; each side bears certain losses automatically.​
- Workers’ Compensation:

- Employer must buy insurance for employees’ work‑related injuries/occupational diseases.​
- Covers:

- Wage loss (up to legislative cap, never more than about 80% of wages).​
- All medical costs related to the workplace injury/disease.​
- Disability benefits (temporary or permanent).
- Death benefit to worker’s estate (in Indiana: 300‑something thousand dollars).​

- Covered if:

- Injury on the job or within scope of employment.
- Worker did not intentionally injure themselves.
- Worker is not under influence of alcohol/drugs.​

- No‑fault auto (a few states like Kentucky):

- Below a threshold amount, each driver pays for their own vehicle damage and injuries, regardless of fault.​
- Traditional negligence rules apply above the threshold.

---

**Trespass (Person, Land, Personal Property)**

- Civil trespass is different from criminal trespass (criminal = government punishes).​
- Trespass to person:

- Physical interference with a person; overlaps with battery and can lead to wrongful death claims by relatives if victim dies.​

- Trespass to land:

- Unauthorized entry or interference with real property (e.g., driving into someone’s house).​
- Negligent damage: liability insurance can pay; intentional damage: often excluded from insurance.​

- Trespass to personal property:

- Interference with movable property (e.g., vandalism, theft, embezzlement, larceny, robbery).​
- Owner can sue for return of property or its value.​

**Defamation: Slander & Libel**

- Defamation elements:

1. False statement of fact.
2. Publication to a third party (heard, seen, or read by someone else).
3. Injury/damages to person or business (reputation, economic loss).​

- Publication: Saying it only to the person defamed (e.g., only to the restaurant owner) is not publication; others must hear/see it.​
- Slander:

- Verbal/oral defamation.​

- Libel:

- Written or pictorial defamation (including online posts, photos, etc.).​

- Intentional nature:

- Insurance typically does not cover defamation because it’s an intentional action, even if speaker thought statement was true.​

Example: Professor mistakenly writes a negative recommendation letter, confusing you with a failing student; you lose a higher‑paying job and accept a job 10,000 less → potential defamation against professor personally, but not against Purdue, and no insurance coverage because intentional publication.​

**Battery (Civil) vs Criminal Battery**

- Civil battery definition:

- Intentional, non‑consensual touching of another in a rude, insolent, or angry manner.​

- Criminal battery:

- Same underlying conduct but prosecuted by the state; penalties include fines/jail/prison.​

- Civil battery remedies:

- Injured person can sue for money damages (medical bills, pain and suffering, etc.).​

- Employment link:

- Normally intentional torts are not covered by employer/insurance, but there’s an exception when the intentional act is within scope of employment.

Example: Club bouncer throws patron out, slamming them onto asphalt and breaking their nose; patron didn’t consent, no legal right to throw them that way → civil battery; both bouncer and club can be jointly and severally liable because removing patrons is part of job (respondeat superior).​

---

**False Imprisonment**

- Definition:

- Holding someone against their will, without legal right, and they are aware there is no reasonable means of escape.​

- Key points:

- Awareness: If the person doesn’t know they’re confined (e.g., doors locked but they never check and are released before noticing), no false imprisonment.​
- Reasonable escape: If a safe, reasonable exit exists (e.g., first‑floor window you can step out of), confinement may not be found.​

Examples:

- Instructor locks classroom doors on first floor and announces you must stay, but windows allow easy exit for able‑bodied students → likely no false imprisonment (reasonable means of escape).​
- Police officer unjustifiably detains a sober 20‑year‑old in the back of a locked squad car with no reasonable escape; civil claim for false imprisonment/false arrest possible, but typical damages may be nominal (e.g., 1) if there’s little actual harm.​

**Wrongful Death & Damages**

- Wrongful death:

- When a person dies due to another’s tortious conduct (intentional, negligent, or strict liability).​
- Relatives bring civil action for monetary damages they suffer from the death.​

- Possible bases:

- Intentional (battery / murder‑type conduct in civil form).
- Negligence (car accidents, stadium collapse).
- Strict liability (ultra‑hazardous activities, defective inherently dangerous products).​

- Damages:

- Compensatory damages: Make plaintiff whole (economic loss like lost income, medical, funeral, plus non‑economic like pain and suffering).​
- Punitive damages: Punish particularly bad conduct and deter others.​

- Economic vs non‑economic value:

- A person making no income may have low “economic” value but still high overall value; juries can use punitive and non‑economic damages to reflect this.​

Example from “The Rainmaker”: A young man with no earnings had “little economic value” under some tort measures, but emotionally and morally his life is obviously worth far more.​
