**Contracts p 2**

Immortal Soul / Online Game Example

- Online game sales page is an invitation to negotiate, not an offer; buyer makes the offer by ordering.​
- Seller may accept or reject; misprints or out-of-stock situations allow refusal.​
- Clicking “I agree” creates voluntary acceptance and an enforceable contract if other elements are met.​
- Consideration exists through mutual promises: seller promises to provide game, buyer promises to pay; actual delivery can come later.​
- Lawful purpose is satisfied by buying a game with money; no illegality in that exchange.​
- Soul term: money + soul together is buyer’s consideration; game is seller’s consideration, so consideration does not depend on valuing the soul.​
- Genuineness of assent issue: “soul” is undefined and reasonable people could disagree on what it is; courts may find no clear assent to that term.​
- Lawful purpose question: there are no known cases on selling one’s “soul,” but if it were deemed illegal or against public policy the soul clause would be unenforceable.​
- Statute of Frauds angle: if a soul is viewed as tied to eternal life (longer than one year), then any contract transferring it would need to be in writing to be enforceable.​

Napkin “Soul for $20” Example

- Writing can be on any medium: napkin, soda can, desktop, email; all count as “in writing” if legible.​
- If other contract elements exist (offer, acceptance, consideration, lawful purpose, capacity, etc.), a napkin can satisfy the “form required by law” for >1‑year contracts.​
- Competency may be challenged if someone is willing to sell their soul; court could treat that as evidence of lack of capacity.​

Thomas’s Driveway Flyers (Minor, Invitation, Vagueness)

- Flyer (“$5 per driveway and $2–$4 for sidewalks, price negotiable”) placed at neighbors’ houses would be an invitation to negotiate, not an offer.​
- Neighbors respond by contacting Thomas; their request plus agreed price becomes the offer which Thomas can accept or counter.​
- If neighbor simply says “Do my driveway and sidewalk” with no further discussion, sidewalk price ($2 vs $3 vs $4) is unclear → lack of genuineness of assent.​
- Once a specific amount is fixed (e.g., $5 driveway + $4 sidewalk = $9), there is genuineness of assent and consideration (services vs money).​
- As a minor (15–16), Thomas can disaffirm the service contract within a reasonable time; adults dealing with him bear that risk.​
- If Thomas completes the work, he is entitled to the agreed $9; if homeowner refuses to pay, that is a breach and homeowner owes damages.​
- Lawful purpose: minors can lawfully earn money shoveling/cleaning; no illegality in the activity itself.​

Goods vs Services; Mixed Contracts; Specialty Goods

- Statute of Frauds: sale of goods ≥ $500 usually must be in writing; pure services only fall under SOF if ≥1 year to perform.​
- Mixed contracts are classified by:

- Law of the jurisdiction (trier-of-fact approach vs. “predominant purpose” test).​
- If labor cost dominates, likely treated as **service**; if goods dominate, treated as **sale of goods**.​

- Teeth cleaning: primarily a service (time and skill) even though dentist uses small amounts of floss/polishing paste.​
- Crown example:

- Involves anesthetic (good), grinding (service), impression (good), lab‑made crown (good), cement (good), installation (service).​
- If cost of goods (e.g., $600 crown plus materials) predominates, transaction may be treated as sale of goods.​

- Specialty/custom goods exception: custom crown or prescription glasses made to one patient’s specification are “specialty goods” that do not need a writing even if ≥ $500.​
- Rationale: seller would not custom-make an unsaleable item without some agreement; courts treat such contracts as enforceable despite SOF writing requirement.​

Waivers, Releases, and Hold Harmless Agreements

- Waiver / release / hold harmless: signer agrees not to sue and often to indemnify (reimburse) the organization if someone sues on their behalf.​
- Consideration: participant gives up the right to sue; organization provides access, equipment, transportation, practice, etc., satisfying “mustard seed” value.​
- Only the party to be bound must sign; Purdue typically does not sign these forms, because they want only the student bound.​
- Examples:

- Purdue lacrosse: waiver to play with University equipment.​
- 22‑caliber shooting at Purdue armory: waiver to limit Purdue’s liability for accidents.​
- Study-abroad program: waiver/release/hold harmless for risks abroad.​
- Paintball company: participant waives right to sue and agrees to indemnify company.​

Counteroffers via Editing Waivers

- Contract drafter’s language is construed against the drafter when ambiguous.​
- If a participant crosses out “not” (turning “will not sue” into “will sue”), and the business still lets them participate, that conduct = acceptance of the counteroffer.​
- Ambiguity example: one clause says “will sue,” another still says “will not sue”; ambiguity is interpreted in favor of the non‑drafter (participant).​
- Practical strategy: subtle edits (non‑red ink, in the middle of a stack) are less likely to be noticed; if accepted, they bind the drafter.​
- High‑risk activities (river rafting, skydiving) often use waivers; operator may refuse service if they notice extensive edits.​

Hospital Release Example and Counteroffer

- Patient asked to sign broad surgical release/authorization while already medicated.​
- Refusal to sign is a rejection of the hospital’s offer; hospital can respond with a counteroffer (narrower terms) or proceed without the waiver.​
- If hospital proceeds with surgery without a signed waiver, their conduct can show acceptance of patient’s counteroffer (no added waiver terms).​

Non‑Compete Agreements

- Non‑compete = employee agrees not to work for competing firms in a defined way after leaving.​
- Generally enforceable in most states (few treat them as illegal), if reasonable under three main limits:

- Time: cannot be excessive; many states cap at 2–4 years.​
- Geographic area: must relate to employer’s actual business area (e.g., 30‑mile radius for a local nail salon, not entire U.S.).​
- Scope of activity: must focus on the type of job/role the employee had (e.g., accounts payable work, specific marketing role), not all jobs.​

- Employees with more experience can sometimes negotiate shorter non‑compete terms because they bring high value to employer.​
- Litigation is expensive: Indiana example ~ $45,000 on average to enforce or defend a non‑compete, so employers may prefer negotiation or deterrence over suit.​
- Some people decline job offers or seek changes to non‑compete terms to avoid being locked out of their field.​

Option Contracts

- Option contract: buyer pays consideration to hold open the right to buy something within a given period; seller must sell if buyer exercises option.​
- Typical use: land/real estate development, such as large retailers (e.g., Walmart) reserving potential expansion sites.​
- Structure:

- First contract = option (seller promises to keep property available; buyer pays option fee).​
- Second contract = actual sale if buyer chooses to proceed during option period.​

- If seller refuses to sell after valid exercise, seller is in breach and owes buyer damages.​

Right of First Refusal

- Right of first refusal (ROFR): holder pays for the right to be offered an asset first if owner ever decides to sell.​
- Key differences from an option:

- Owner has no duty to sell at all; only must offer to ROFR holder first if they decide to sell.​
- ROFR holder can decide at that time whether to buy on the agreed terms (fixed price or appraised value formula).​

- Genuineness of assent: contract often specifies how price will be determined (e.g., appraisal by a named company) to avoid vagueness.​

Firm Offers (UCC 2‑205)

- Firm offer is a UCC rule modifying common law; applies only to merchants and the sale of goods.​
- Requirements:

- Offer by a merchant to buy or sell goods.​
- In a signed writing (signature can be typed name or initials showing intent).​
- States that it will be held open.​
- Irrevocable for the stated time (or reasonable time) not exceeding three months.​

- No consideration from the offeree is required to hold the offer open (unlike an option contract).​
- JC Penney blinds example: written quote with item numbers, prices, and expiration date; signed by salesperson = firm offer.​
- If merchant refuses to honor firm offer and buyer must pay higher price elsewhere, buyer’s damages = price difference.​

Auctions: With Reserve vs Without Reserve

- Auctioneer’s “Do I hear $5?” is an invitation to negotiate; bidders make offers.​
- Auction with reserve (most common): auctioneer is free not to accept the highest bid; no obligation to sell.​
- Auction without reserve (often called “absolute”): auctioneer must accept the highest bid; last bid forms a contract.​
- If auctioneer were treated as making the offers, rejecting lower bids in favor of higher ones would create multiple breaches; thus law treats audience bids as offers.​
- All usual elements still required: identifiable item (genuineness of assent), consideration (money vs goods), capacity, lawful purpose, and any necessary writing if SOF applies.​

Unilateral vs Bilateral Contracts

- Bilateral contract: mutual exchange of promises; acceptance is a promise. Almost all examples in class are bilateral.​
- Unilateral contract: acceptance occurs only by performance, not by a promise.​
- Examples:

- Lost dog reward: only paying if someone actually returns the dog.​
- Crime tips reward: paying only if info leads to arrest/conviction.​
- Grass cutting: “I will pay you if you show up Saturday at 8 and finish by noon” – no contract until completed performance.​

- Businesses sometimes use unilateral structures when they distrust a potential counterparty and only want obligation if performance actually occurs.​

Damages in Contract Law

- Compensatory (compensative) damages: put the injured party in the position they would have been in but for the breach (difference in price, extra costs, etc.).​
- Punitive damages: designed to punish and deter particularly egregious conduct; rare in contract cases and usually tied to separate torts (fraud, bad faith).​
- Pain and suffering: not available for breach of contract alone; belongs to tort law, not contract law.​

Liquidated Damages

- Liquidated damages clause specifies in advance what damages will be if a party breaches.​
- Enforceable if the amount is a reasonable forecast of expected harm and not a penalty.​
- GM employment example: $35,000 (plus 8% interest) owed if employee quits within 12 months; justified by high training and onboarding cost (~$40,000+).​
- Construction/home example: homeowner may include per‑day or lump‑sum liquidated damages if builder fails to finish by agreed date.​
- Home sale contract: seller may impose a fixed amount (e.g., $5,000) as liquidated damages if buyer backs out, even if seller later sells for more.​
- Corporate “breakup fees” in mergers (e.g., $900 million if Sallie Mae merger fails) are liquidated damages; labels don’t matter, function does.​

Specific Performance

- Specific performance is an equitable remedy requiring the breaching party to deliver the unique thing promised rather than paying money.​
- Typically unavailable for mass‑produced goods; money damages are considered adequate.​
- Used for unique items: real estate, rare collectibles, unique art, one‑of‑a‑kind goods where fair market value is hard to measure and money is inadequate.​

Mechanic Shop Extreme Example (Risk Allocation)

- Customer agrees to pay $3,500 for transmission work; shop finishes work, throws away old parts, and locks car in secure shop.​
- Thief breaks in overnight and steals car; shop had high security and acted reasonably → no negligence.​
- Contract was performed: shop provided agreed repair; customer must still pay even though car was stolen before pickup.​
- Dispute over theft is a tort issue; without negligence, shop is not liable in tort either.​

Parol Evidence Rule

- When contract is written, courts generally exclude prior or contemporaneous oral statements that contradict or vary the written terms.​
- Exceptions: evidence of fraud, mistake, deception, or misrepresentation may be admissible to challenge or interpret the writing.​
- Goal: encourage clear written agreements and avoid unreliable “he said, she said” testimony.​

Ambiguity and Interpretation Against Drafter

- Ambiguous language in a contract is interpreted against the party who drafted it.​
- Crossed‑out terms that create conflicting provisions (e.g., one clause says “will sue,” another says “will not sue”) produce ambiguity resolved in favor of non‑drafter.​

Past Consideration (Past Benefits)

- Promise to pay for something already received, with no new benefit given, is not supported by consideration.​
- Debt collection example: debtor owes $10,000; creditor offers to forgive $6,000 if debtor pays $4,000.​

- Debtor gives no new consideration; still legally owes remaining $6,000.​

- Employment example: employer promises extra $10,000 to induce professor not to quit mid‑semester.​

- If professor just completes duties already owed, there is no new consideration; employer is not legally bound to pay the extra.​
- Even small additional duties (e.g., teaching extra time) can supply new consideration and make the promise enforceable.​

Written vs Printed vs Typed Terms

- Handwritten terms override pre‑printed boilerplate; printed terms override typed terms when there is a conflict.​
- Reason: handwritten changes are presumed to be the latest and most deliberate expressions of agreement.​
- Institutions often ask parties to initial changes, but legally a pre‑signature handwritten change can be effective without initials.​

Franchises

- Franchise = contract authorizing franchisee to use franchisor’s brand, system, and intellectual property (e.g., McDonald’s).​
- Governed by general contract principles plus specialized statutes/regulations, but fundamentally still a contract with all standard elements.​
