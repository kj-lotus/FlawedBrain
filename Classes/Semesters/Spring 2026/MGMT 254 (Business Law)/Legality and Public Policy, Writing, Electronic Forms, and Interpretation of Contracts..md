## 1. Legality and Public Policy

A contract must have a **legal** purpose and not violate public policy to be enforceable.

## A. Illegal agreements (void)

- Agreements to commit crimes or torts (e.g., paying someone to assault a competitor) are void.
    
- Agreements that violate licensing statutes:
    
    - Regulatory licenses (protect public, e.g., attorneys, doctors): unlicensed contracts usually unenforceable.
        
    - Revenue‑raising licenses (primarily to raise money): contracts often still enforceable.
        
- Gambling/wagering agreements that don’t comply with state law are generally void.
    
- “Sunday”/usury statutes:
    
    - Usury = charging more than the legal maximum interest rate; remedies vary by statute (forfeit interest, part of principal, or entire loan).
        

## B. Agreements contrary to public policy

Even if not criminal, some bargains conflict with societal interests.

- Agreements that obstruct justice: paying witnesses to testify falsely, suppressing evidence, bribing officials.
    
- Agreements injuring public service: contracts that unduly restrict government employees or interfere with official duties.
    
- “Unconscionable” agreements: so one‑sided they shock the conscience; courts may refuse to enforce all or part (procedural unconscionability = unfair bargaining process, substantive = overly harsh terms).
    

## C. Noncompete / restraint of trade

- General rule: Contracts that unreasonably restrain trade are void; “reasonable” restraints may be upheld.
    
- Employment noncompete: enforceable if
    
    - Protects a legitimate business interest (trade secrets, customer goodwill),
        
    - Reasonable in time, geography, and scope of activity,
        
    - Not unduly harsh to employee or injurious to the public.
        
- Sale‑of‑business noncompete: more likely enforced—seller can be restricted from competing in the same area for a reasonable time.
    

## D. Effect of illegality

- General rule: courts leave parties “where they find them”; no recovery for breach of an illegal contract.
    
- Possible exceptions allowing some recovery:
    
    - Party not equally at fault (less culpable, protected class).
        
    - Divisible contract: legal part may be enforced if it can be separated from illegal part.
        
    - Withdrawal before illegal act occurs.[](https://www.bobfarley.us/0300lawclasses/315businesslaw/Books/Andersons%20Business%20Law.pdf)​

## 2. Writing and the Statute of Frauds

Chapter 16 focuses on when contracts must be in **writing**, how electronic forms work, and how courts interpret unclear terms.

## A. Validity of oral contracts

- Oral contracts are generally valid and enforceable unless a statute requires a writing.
    
- Problem is proof: “Statute of Frauds” lists categories requiring written evidence.
    

## B. Typical Statute of Frauds categories

A writing is required for:

1. **Contracts for the sale of land or interests in land** (including leases beyond a certain duration, mortgages, easements).
    
2. **Contracts that cannot by their terms be performed within one year** from the date made (if possible to perform within one year, no writing required).
    
3. **Promises to answer for the debt or default of another** (suretyship/guaranty contracts) except the “main purpose” rule (if the promisor’s primary object is their own economic benefit, writing may not be required).
    
4. **Promises made in consideration of marriage** (e.g., prenuptial agreements).
    
5. **Contracts for the sale of goods price $500 or more** (UCC requirement; amount may vary by statute).
    

## C. Requirements of the writing

- There must be a “note or memorandum” that:
    
    - Identifies the parties,
        
    - States the essential terms (e.g., subject matter, quantity for goods),
        
    - Is signed by the party to be charged (the one resisting enforcement).
        
- Multiple documents can be read together if they clearly relate to the same transaction.
    

## D. UCC special rules (goods)

- Writing may be very informal as long as it indicates a contract and states quantity.
    
- Between merchants, a written confirmation sent and not objected to within a reasonable time can satisfy Statute of Frauds against the recipient.
    

## E. Effect of non‑compliance

- Contract is unenforceable (not void), meaning it cannot be enforced in court against a party who raises the Statute of Frauds defense.
    
- Exceptions: part performance, admissions in court, specially manufactured goods, or reliance may allow some enforcement.

## 3. Electronic forms and signatures

Anderson treats electronic contracts and signatures as functionally equivalent to paper, consistent with E‑SIGN and UETA.

## A. Enforceability of e‑contracts

- A contract made by email, website order, or click‑through can satisfy “writing” requirements.
    
- “Click‑wrap” and “browse‑wrap” agreements can be binding if terms are reasonably presented and assent is manifested (e.g., clicking “I agree”).
    

## B. Electronic signatures

- An electronic symbol or process attached to a record and adopted by a person with intent to sign counts as a signature (typing your name, clicking a box, digital certificate, etc.).
    
- Statutes generally prohibit denying legal effect solely because a record or signature is electronic.
    

## C. Record retention

- Businesses must be able to retain and accurately reproduce electronic records for later reference; this usually satisfies legal “record” requirements.

## 4. Interpretation of contracts

When terms are disputed or ambiguous, courts apply rules of interpretation to determine the parties’ **intent**.

## A. Primary goal

- The main objective is to ascertain and give effect to the intent of the parties as expressed in the contract language and surrounding circumstances.[](https://scholarship.law.georgetown.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2971&context=facpub)​
    

## B. Rules of construction (typical hierarchy)

1. **Plain meaning rule**:
    
    - If language is clear and unambiguous, courts give words their ordinary meaning and do not look to external evidence.
        
2. **Specific vs. general**:
    
    - Specific terms control over general terms when they conflict.[](https://www.bobfarley.us/0300lawclasses/315businesslaw/Books/Andersons%20Business%20Law.pdf)​
        
3. **Handwritten/typed vs. printed**:
    
    - Handwritten terms control over typed, and typed terms control over preprinted boilerplate, on the theory they reflect more specific intent.[](https://www.bobfarley.us/0300lawclasses/315businesslaw/Books/Andersons%20Business%20Law.pdf)​
        
4. **Ambiguities construed against drafter** (“contra proferentem”):
    
    - Ambiguous language is interpreted against the party who drafted the contract, especially in adhesion contracts.
        

## C. Use of external context

- Courts may consider:
    
    - Course of performance (how the parties have performed this contract),
        
    - Course of dealing (how they acted in previous contracts),
        
    - Usage of trade (industry customs).
        
- These help fill gaps or clarify ambiguous terms without contradicting clear language.
    

## D. Parol evidence rule (brief)

- If the parties have a final written contract, prior or contemporaneous oral agreements generally cannot be used to change its terms.
    
- Exceptions: evidence of fraud, duress, mistake, lack of capacity, or to explain ambiguities or show subsequent modifications.

## 5. Quick exam checklist

When you see a contracts question touching these topics, move through this checklist.

1. **Legality/public policy**
    
    - Does the subject matter involve crime, tort, unlicensed activity, usury, gambling, restraint of trade, or unconscionable terms?
        
    - Is there a noncompete—are time, geography, and scope reasonable?
        
2. **Statute of Frauds**
    
    - Does the contract fall in a category requiring writing (land, >1 year, surety, marriage, goods ≥ threshold)?
        
    - Is there some signed memorandum with essential terms? Any exception (part performance, admission, specially manufactured goods)?
        
3. **Electronic form**
    
    - Is there an electronic record and electronic signature showing intent to be bound?
        
    - Was the user given reasonable notice of terms (especially in click‑wrap/browse‑wrap)?
        
4. **Interpretation**
    
    - Is language ambiguous? If yes, apply: plain meaning, specific over general, handwritten over printed, construed against drafter.
        
    - Can course of dealing, performance, or trade usage clarify?
