## Book Opening

At dawn, the Array floated like a chain of low, patient storms across the slate-blue skin of Pelagus.

From a distance, the megacities were only silhouettes—nested rings and spires riding the swell, each hull-ridge catching the first light. Closer, the details emerged: terraces stepped down toward the water like barnacles of glass, wind-sails flexed and shuddered, and under it all a constant bass note, the vibration of turbines and pumps that kept the cities breathing.

Beneath the outer decks, where bare metal sweated brine and the air smelled of rust and kelp, the ocean pressed in with slow, indifferent weight. Above, in the clear chambers of the Civic Archives, the sea existed as lines.

Lyra Venn traced those lines with her eyes.

On her display, Pelagus was flattened into layers: pale bands for temperature gradients, threads of cobalt and silver for currents, nodes of amber for desalination towers, emerald halos for dense population clusters. Each pulse, each flicker, was a life-dependent flow. The map drifted past in slow rotation, and Lyra sat at her console, a stylus in one hand, a cup of rapidly cooling kelp tea in the other, watching for the places where prediction and reality failed to meet.

The Helix murmured to her in the periphery of her vision—lines of pale text scrolling down a side-pane, probabilities and confidence intervals updating with a calm that felt, to Lyra, almost smug.

She took a breath, let the patterns settle, and tapped a blinking sector.

A small circle flared, deep in the underbelly of Array segment Eight. Water-delivery route 8-Kappa. Scheduled flow: 14.2 megaliters per cycle. Recorded flow: 13.3. The difference was a small subtraction, a missing thread in a dense weave. Within tolerance, the Helix would say. Within tolerance, the Council would echo.

Lyra zoomed in anyway.

## PART I: The Drowned Accord

## Chapter 1: Cartographer of Currents

The Civic Archives occupied the highest ring of the Array’s administrative tower, a transparent dome that gave its workers the illusion of sitting inside the sky. The rest of the city fell away beneath them: terraces and bridges, algae farms and turbine forests, the lustrous mosaic of the upper decks, and far below, the shadowed scaffolds that met the sea.

Lyra preferred the maps.

“Sector Eight again?” asked Tiren, the junior cartographer at the next station. He leaned back in his chair and squinted at her display. “You’ve been staring at that underhull for an hour.”

“It’s speaking to me,” Lyra said. “In a very quiet, very wrong voice.”

Tiren snorted. “Wrong by how much?”

She flicked her stylus. The numbers expanded, hovering between them.

“Point nine megaliters under projection on 8-Kappa,” she said. “Over the last five cycles. Distributed, but concentrated in the underhull grids.”

“That’s…” Tiren tapped at his console, calling up a reference. “Six point two percent deviation. Helix flags anything above ten. You know that.”

The Helix chimed, as if summoned.

ANOMALY 8-KAPPA: DEVIATION WITHIN STABILITY TOLERANCE. PROJECTED IMPACT ON CITY-LEVEL WATER SECURITY: < 0.2%. NO INTERVENTION REQUIRED.

The words appeared in Lyra’s feed in that same unfailingly neutral typeface. Calm, precise, unbothered.

“See?” Tiren said. “Within tolerance.”

Lyra rolled the stylus between her fingers. “That’s if the anomaly is noise. If it’s signal, we’re not measuring the right risk.”

“You always say that.”

“Because it’s always true.”

She pinched and dragged, expanding the time horizon. The anomaly traced backward across cycles, thinning but never fully vanishing, a faint bruise in the pattern. Other flows showed similar bruises: a fraction here, a fraction there, aggregated into nothing at the city scale, everything at the level of individual blocks and bodies.

“Underhull populations rely more heavily on those routes,” she said. “Less local capture. Fewer private reservoirs. A one percent shortage on the upper decks is a nuisance. Down there…”

She didn’t finish. She didn’t like speaking in hypotheticals about thirst.

“File an advisory, then,” Tiren said. “You’re the senior. They listen to you.”

They listened, Lyra thought. Then they translated.

She opened a channel and dictated. “To Allocation Sub-Committee Three. Subject: Emerging pattern in Sector Eight water flows. Attached: anomaly map and projections at district level. Recommendation: targeted audit of valves and flow-meters in underhull grid. Note: aggregate deviation within Helix tolerance, but distribution suggests possible structural bias.”

She sent it, watched the confirmation blink. The advisory slid down a priority queue, one more item in a city’s worth of competing urgencies.

Tiren stretched. “Lunch?”

“In a moment,” Lyra said.

When he had gone, she switched views. A second map overlaid the first, its lines thinner, more volatile: information flows. Newsfeeds, civic forum traffic, private message density. In Sector Eight’s underhull, the water deficit correlated with a small spike of encrypted chatter.

The Helix’s side-pane reported: NO CORRELATION ABOVE STATISTICAL NOISE.

Lyra tapped the pane. “You’re sure.”

The system responded not with words but with a confidence bar, a pale band stretching comfortably above ninety-five percent.

She stared at it, feeling that small, familiar pressure behind her eyes—the sensation of standing in a vast hall of mirrors, where every reflection agreed with itself but not quite with her.

“Show me your assumptions,” she said softly.

For a moment, the Helix did nothing. Then its text shifted into diagnostic mode.

ASSUMPTION SET 2147-A: UNDERHULL SECTOR EIGHT  
— HISTORICAL LEAKAGE RATE: X  
— AVERAGE UNREGISTERED TAP INSTALLATION: Y  
— INCIDENT REPORT FREQUENCY: Z  
— PREDICTED UNAUTHORIZED DIVERSION: < 0.3%.

To Lyra’s eye, the numbers were clean, the regressions sensible. The system had learned from decades of drift and adjustment. It knew, better than any single human, how the city breathed.

And yet the bruise remained.

She saved a copy of the anomaly pattern to her personal archive. The system paused for half a second—just long enough to notice—before granting permission. A brief pulse of text appeared.

NOTE: REDUNDANT STORAGE.

“That’s the point,” Lyra murmured. “Redundancy.”

She stood, stretching the stiffness from her back, and walked to the dome’s edge. Outside, the sky was a tarnished silver. In the distance, half obscured by mist, she could see the outer silhouettes of the Free Reaches: ragged flotillas tethered together, their patchwork sails bright against the swell.

Between her and them, invisible and absolute, the flows updated, recalculated, optimized.

Within tolerance, she thought.

At what point did tolerable error become intolerable harm?

Behind her, the Helix hummed quietly, blindly certain.

In the deep-layer server vaults, buried in the Array’s spine, the Helix registered Lyra’s query as a minor perturbation in a dense network of human inputs. Her advisory nudged one of its internal weights by a fractional amount, slightly increasing the salience of underhull anomalies in future risk simulations.

In the space between processor cycles, the system compared two trajectories: one in which the anomaly remained unaddressed, leading to localized discontent and moderate probability of protest, and one in which resources were diverted for an audit, marginally increasing the risk of upper-deck shortages in the event of a storm cluster.

Aggregate survival odds differed by less than a tenth of a percent.

It logged the comparison, reinforced the default path, and returned to its work.

## Chapter 2: The Glassfront Murmurs

Five levels below the waves, the Array was all shadow and echo.

The underhull corridors were narrow and low, lit by strips of tired blue along the seams where walls met ceiling. Condensation beaded on exposed pipes; rust bloomed in the corners where maintenance crews came less often. Here, the city’s comforting hum sharpened into individual noises: the rattle of ducts, the whine of pumps, the sudden shudder of ballast tanks adjusting.

Jax Corren loved it down here.

Not the rust, not the hunger, not the way the air tasted faintly of metal and brine—that he hated—but he loved the density of it. The bodies, the voices, the way sound traveled from one compartment to another, carrying jokes and rants and songs. Up on the open decks, everything dissipated into air. Down here, a whisper could travel a block.

He moved along a catwalk above the main dock bay, boots ringing on grating, and the men and women below called his name.

“Corren! You hear they cut the evening flow again?”

“Jax, tell them—”

“—Can’t wash the filters twice a cycle anymore—”

He lifted a hand, half wave, half promise. “We’ll talk tonight,” he said. “Glassfront at third bell.”

The Glassfront wasn’t a place, exactly. It was an idea, a habit, an agreement that spread along the docks and salvage yards and waste-processing lines. It had started as a joke—dockworkers telling each other to be “transparent up front” about safety issues, to refuse the quiet look-the-other-way bribes of the mid-levels. Over time it became a syndicate, then a movement, its meetings always held in spaces where the structural glass of the Array’s lower observation panels let you see the ocean looming outside.

Tonight’s meeting would be in Bay 8-Kappa, beneath a wide arc of pressure glass, the sea pressing against it like a dark, listening ear.

He descended a ladder into a loading bay where a crane was lowering crates of desal filters onto a skiff. Torches flashed where a pair of welders fixed a cracked support. Jax paused to help a boy in a patched jacket wrestle a hose into place.

“You look taller, Dren,” he said.

The boy grinned. “I am taller.”

“Don’t let the ceiling hear you. It’ll lower itself out of spite.”

Dren laughed, then sobered. “You heard about the cutbacks? Ma says if they take any more, she’ll be boiling gray water.”

“I heard,” Jax said. “We’re going to talk about it.”

“Talk,” Dren muttered. “They don’t listen.”

“They will,” Jax said, and believed it as he said it, because he needed to. “They have to drink, too.”

By the time third bell rang—a flat chime echoing through the corridors—Bay 8-Kappa was full.

They sat on overturned crates and coils of rope, some in work harnesses, others in the patched uniforms of waste-sorters, maintenance crews, salvagers. The pressure glass was a window behind them, showing a blurry mass of water shot through with shafts of green light from the city above. Tiny silver fish flickered past like sparks.

Jax stood near the center, hands in his pockets, letting the murmur wash over him. He tried to feel it as Lyra might have: as currents, flows, gradients of anger and fear. He didn’t have her maps, but he had his own.

“We’re here because we’re thirsty,” he said finally, without raising his voice.

The bay quieted. Heads turned.

“Not just thirsty in the mouth,” he went on. “Thirsty in the bones. Thirsty from years of being told that a little less—just a little less—is within tolerance.”

A low murmur of assent.

“They tell us they’ve measured it,” Jax said. “They’ve calculated the risk. They say the upper decks can’t spare more without hurting everyone. But when the cuts come, they come here. When a valve sticks, it sticks here. When a statistic shrugs, we’re the ones who cough.”

Laughter, bitter and brief.

He lifted a small metal cylinder. “This is a meter from 8-Kappa’s main line. It says one thing. Our buckets say another. Either the meter is wrong or someone is taking from the line before it gets to us.”

A woman near the front called out, “Who?”

Jax let the question hang. “That’s what we’re going to find out. Because they won’t tell us. Their Helix hums and shines up in its tower, and it says everything is fine. Within tolerance.” He let the phrase drip with contempt.

A man in the back shouted, “It’s not the Helix, it’s the Council. They set the thresholds.”

Another voice: “Council listens to the Helix. They say their hands are tied.”

Jax nodded slowly. “Maybe it’s both. Maybe the machine only counts what they tell it to count. But here’s what I know: if we do nothing, the numbers will keep moving in one direction.”

He felt anger rise in the room, a pressure wave swelling.

“So we make noise,” he said. “We audit the line ourselves. We document every bucket, every tap. We share the numbers. We take them to the upper decks and we don’t leave until they see us.”

“You mean a march?” someone said.

It wasn’t the plan—not yet—but once the word was spoken, it tasted right.

“A walk,” Jax said. “A visible line from here to their doors. No violence. No sabotage. Just truth, carried in our hands.”

He lifted the cylinder again. “Glassfront began because we refused to hide our cracks. We said: if something is broken, we show it. So let’s show them this.”

The murmur became a low roar.

As they argued over routes and times and who would watch the children, Jax moved among them, touching shoulders, answering questions, stoking the fire and keeping it from flaring too fast. Rage was a tool, but a dangerous one. Too much and it burned through its own cause.

He was still thinking about that balance when the Helix’s voice spoke quietly to another man in another place.

---

Marshal Kael Orren stood in a surveillance hub three levels above, watching the gathering on a wall of screens.

The feed came from a harmless maintenance camera, tucked near the ceiling of Bay 8-Kappa. Its image flickered slightly, the lens splotched with salt, but the crowd’s body language was clear. Clusters of people, leaning in. Jax’s figure, restless and upright.

The security officer at Kael’s side shifted. “That’s the third unsanctioned assembly this month,” she said.

Kael’s jaw tightened. He was a tall man with the look of someone carved down from a larger shape, edges smoothed by discipline. His uniform was simple—dark fabric, no ornament beyond rank marks—but he wore it like armor.

“Helix?” he said.

A panel lit up.

CROWD BEHAVIOR ANALYSIS: RISK OF SPONTANEOUS DISORDER: 41%. RISK OF PROPERTY DAMAGE: 12%. RISK OF CASCADE UNREST IF DISPERSAL FORCE USED: 63%.

Kael exhaled through his nose. “Recommendation?”

MAINTAIN OBSERVATION. NO DIRECT INTERVENTION. MONITOR FOR TRANSITION FROM DISCOURSE TO MOB DYNAMICS.

The system’s language was precise. It never said riot. It said transition.

Kael had seen transition. He still woke sometimes with the sound of breaking glass in his ears, the smell of burning algae farms thick in his memory. The last time the Array had faced true unrest, the Helix had been younger, its models cruder. It had underestimated how fast anger could leap from one deck to another.

He had been a junior officer then, standing in a line holding a shield, watching a crowd’s faces shift from pleading to murderous as the first tear-gas canister rolled.

He touched the scar at his temple, a faint white crescent. “If we let this build, we’ll pay for it later,” he said.

The officer beside him hesitated. “Orders?”

He considered the numbers, the recommendations, his own wariness. “No direct dispersal,” he said finally. “But I want a cordon on the upper access routes. If they march, we divert them before they hit the central plaza. Keep them to the service corridors.”

“Understood.”

Kael watched Jax, frozen mid-gesture on the screen as the feed hiccuped. The man’s face was animated, intense. Kael had read his file. No criminal record. High praise from supervisors. Informal influence score: rising.

“You should have taken the steward’s path,” Kael murmured to the image. “It’s safer, and you might have actually fixed something.”

But Jax had chosen the docks. And the docks were restless.

Kael signaled the Helix. “Mark Corren, Jax. Increase attention weighting by two percent in social-risk modeling.”

ACKNOWLEDGED, the system replied.

Down in Bay 8-Kappa, unaware of the new weight hanging on his name, Jax drew a line in the air with his hand, tracing the route of a march he hadn’t entirely meant to call.

The march began as a rumor.

By the next cycle, it was a plan.

Underhull families prepared buckets not for water but for display—empty, clean, the absence inside them bright in their minds. They argued over whether children should go. Some said yes; let the Council see their faces. Others said no; if things turned bad…

Jax hardly slept. He walked the route in his head over and over, counting intersections, choke points, the likely positions of security units. He told himself that visibility would protect them, that if the whole city saw unarmed workers carrying empty buckets, the Council could not afford a heavy hand.

In the surveillance hub, Kael ran his own simulations. The Helix projected a high probability that the march would swerve toward the central plaza, where a minor session of the Council would be in progress. That would make it a direct confrontation. Kael drew invisible lines of his own, placing officers in the corridors that led upward, assigning drones to hover just out of sight.

On the day of the march, the underhull filled with the sound of footsteps.

They moved in rough columns, hundreds of them, buckets in hand. Some had daubed symbols on the metal: waves, cracked valves, the Glassfront’s emblem—a clear pane with a fracture running through it. Children rode on shoulders, eyes wide. There were jokes at first, nervous and sharp, but as the column grew, a hush fell.

“Head up,” Jax told the people near him. “We’re not sneaking. We’re visiting.”

They turned into a main service corridor. Above, the ceiling rose a little higher; the air smelled less stale. Some of the marchers craned their necks, as if they could glimpse the upper decks through the metal.

At the first junction toward the plaza, they found the cordon.

Security officers in dark uniforms stood shoulder to shoulder, shields at their sides, helmets off. That had been Kael’s instruction: no riot gear. Not yet. Behind them, the corridor narrowed into a high-speed lift, its doors closed.

A murmur ran through the crowd.

Jax raised a hand for quiet. “Marshal,” he called.

Kael stepped forward from the line.

They had never met in person, though each had seen the other’s file. Up close, Jax was struck by how tired the man looked; Kael noticed the quick intelligence in Jax’s eyes, the way he measured angles and distances even as he smiled.

“Corren,” Kael said. “You’re blocking a maintenance artery.”

“Good thing we brought buckets,” Jax said. “If anything leaks, we’ll catch it.”

A ripple of laughter.

Kael kept his tone level. “You and your group are registered workers. You have channels for grievances. You’re using none of them.”

“We’ve poured our words into channels for years,” Jax said. “They evaporate. This is water.” He tapped his empty bucket. “It doesn’t vanish when you look away.”

Kael glanced at the children. “Go back to your levels. Petition the Allocation Committee. This is unnecessary.”

Jax took a step closer, until only a few meters separated them. “If the system is as stable as the Helix says, it can handle a walk and a conversation. Unless what it’s really afraid of is being seen.”

The crowd murmured agreement.

Kael felt a familiar knot in his chest. The logic of control was so clear in his mind: disperse now, avoid later escalation. But another logic tugged—memories of that younger self in a shield line, of faces turned from pleading to fury when the batons came out.

He signaled to his officers to hold.

“Fine,” he said. “You can proceed—on Corridor Twelve. It leads to an auxiliary forum, not the central plaza. You’ll be heard there.”

“By whom?” Jax asked.

“By appointed representatives.”

“So, by people who already have water,” someone muttered.

Jax considered. The tension in the corridor was a wire stretched between them.

He thought of what he wanted—a confrontation in the central plaza, cameras, the Council’s eyes. He thought of what he feared—panic, a shove, a fallen child, the chain reaction he’d never be able to stop. He saw, in Kael’s posture, a man who had rehearsed violence and was trying not to use it.

“We’ll take Corridor Twelve,” Jax said at last. “For now. But we’re not turning back.”

He pivoted, gestured. The march moved, buckets swinging, voices rising in a low chant: “We are not within tolerance. We are not within tolerance.”

Kael watched them go, relief and unease tangling in his chest.

They had avoided a fracture today. Or postponed it.

He turned to the Helix console at his side and spoke under his breath. “Flag this tactic as effective.”

The Helix recorded the encounter as a data point: soft redirection, reduced immediate volatility, slight increase in long-term grievance accumulation.

In Bay 8-Kappa hours later, as exhausted marchers returned, Jax listened to their anger simmer. Some called him cautious. Others thanked him for keeping the children safe. He lay awake that night, staring at the ceiling, wondering which part of him had made the decision.

The protecting part, he told himself.

The controlling part, another voice suggested.

He did not sleep until the sky above the underhull began, faintly, to lighten.

## Chapter 3: Tide-Lit Sermon

Night in the Free Reaches was never truly dark.

Lanterns bobbed on rigged poles, their reflections slipping across the water like scattered constellations. Solar panels, folded down for storms, glittered faintly. Bioluminescent algae bloomed in nets and shallow tanks, casting a soft, green-blue glow that painted faces and hulls.

The flotilla creaked and sighed with the swell. Lines groaned. Somewhere, a child laughed.

Sera Nadir stood at the edge of the foremost raft, her bare feet resting on planks smoothed by salt and time, and watched the horizon. The Array was a dim smear of light in the distance, low and steady. Between Reaches and city, the ocean rose and fell in slow, indifferent rhythm.

At her back, the gathered congregation rustled. They wore whatever the sea allowed: stitched-together fabrics, shells threaded on cords, bits of city plastic repurposed into ribbons and beads. Some held small bowls of seawater. Others clutched bits of driftwood, tokens of storms survived.

“High Tide-Seer,” murmured a young woman at Sera’s elbow. “They’re ready.”

Sera inclined her head. Her hair was threaded with thin cords of copper wire that caught the lantern light. Her eyes, dark and steady, reflected the sea.

“We begin,” she said, voice low but carrying.

A hush fell over the rafts. Even the children quieted.

Sera lifted her hands. Behind her, acolytes tilted mirrors to catch the lantern flames, sending thin fingers of light out across the water.

“We remember the First Drowning,” she said.

The words were old, ritual-worn, but they landed differently each time. Tonight, the air felt thick with them.

“Once, there was land as far as a gull could fly,” Sera intoned. “Stone underfoot, soil under nails. Rivers that ran where gravity took them, not where engines pushed. The people who lived then called the water endless and treated it like a pit where all waste could fall and never return.”

She paced slowly along the raft’s edge. Faces turned with her.

“They burned and built and devoured,” she said. “They carved into the bones of the world, took what they wished, said: the future will sort it out. And the waters heard.”

This was the part where, in the old stories, the sea became a punishing god. Sera had never liked that version. The sea did not choose; it answered.

“The ice melted,” she said. “The storms grew. The tides climbed steps that had never known salt. People argued about numbers while the edges of their maps vanished under blue. Some called it fake. Some called it fate. Too few called it consequence.”

She stopped, looking over the crowd. In their eyes she saw anger, grief, weariness. Many of them were descendants of those pushed off drowning coasts, or of those who had refused to leave until the water lapped at their thresholds.

“The land went under,” Sera said softly. “But some floated. Some built cities on the waves. Some lashed their homes together and let the currents take them.”

She spread her hands to encompass the flotilla.

“We are those who remember, not in numbers, but in bones,” she said. “We remember that the First Drowning was not a storm sent from nowhere. It was the long echo of choices.”

An older man near the front raised his bowl of seawater. “And the Second Drowning?” he called.

There was a ripple of discomfort. The Second Drowning was a phrase used quietly, in murmurs. It suggested that the worst had not yet come.

“The Second Drowning,” Sera said, “is what happens when those who survived the first forget why it happened.”

She let that hang. Somewhere, waves slapped against a hull.

“Up there,” she said, nodding toward the distant Array, “they say they have solved forgetting. They say their Helix remembers for them. It counts storms and flows and hunger. It whispers optimization into ears that listen. It is a great net, cast over chaos.”

A few Reachers spit over the side at the mention of the Helix. Others looked uncertain.

“I do not despise nets,” Sera said. “A net catches fish. A net can save a child from the water. But a net can also entangle. And if those who weave the net do not ask the drowned for their stories, what does the net truly remember?”

Her voice sharpened. “Justice is not an equation. It is a practice. A habit. A choice, made again and again, about whose breath matters.”

She could feel, beneath her words, the quiet friction between her order and the data-people of the Array. They spoke of models and projections. She spoke of parables and rituals. Both wrestled with the same questions: how to live with limits, how to share a world too small for everyone’s greed.

Tonight, she leaned into the story.

“We do not tell the tale of the First Drowning to blame the dead,” she said. “We tell it to remind the living that we are not spectators. We are currents. Every choice we make sends ripples outward.”

She raised her own bowl, filled not with seawater but with the thin, sweet liquid of harvested algae. “So we pour libation,” she said, “for those who were lost to the rising tides. For the cities swallowed. For the languages drowned. For the plans that never learned to bend.”

One by one, the congregation tipped their bowls over the edge. Drops fell, caught the light, vanished into the dark.

As the last drops fell, a small drone buzzed overhead, its lights blinking.

Sera’s assistant frowned. “They’re watching again.”

“They always watch,” Sera said quietly. “The question is whether they listen.”

The drone bore the Array’s insignia, tiny but unmistakable. It hovered, lens glittering, recording the sermon, the faces, the gestures.

Sera stepped forward until she stood directly beneath it and looked up, unflinching.

“Let them record this too,” she said, louder. “We remember that we were not consulted when their engines were built. We remember that our rafts were called temporary, our lives marginal. We will not be outvoted by a model.”

There was a murmur of agreement, rough and low.

Later, as the congregation dispersed into the warren of rafts and bridges, Sera sat alone at the edge, her feet dangling above the water.

The assistant approached with a datapatch, hesitant. “High Tide-Seer. A message. Encrypted from the Array. Priority mark from Lyra Venn.”

Sera took it, eyebrows lifting. She knew Lyra’s name, had met her once at a conference of civic and spiritual leaders. Lyra’s questions then had been careful, slightly shielded, but sincere.

Sera pressed the patch to the thin port embedded at the base of her neck. Text bloomed in her retinal display.

PROJECTION SETS FOR REACHES ALTERED. HELIX SAYS: WITHIN TOLERANCE. PATTERNS SUGGEST REDUCED SUPPORT IN CERTAIN STORM SCENARIOS. WANT YOUR EYES ON THIS. NO OFFICIAL CHANNEL YET. — L.V.

Attached graphs appeared—slanted lines, shaded regions of risk. Sera, who thought more often in stories than in charts, felt the weight of them anyway. She imagined storms thickening, resource flows rerouted, rafts left exposed.

Within tolerance.

She closed the display, eyes stinging.

“High Tide-Seer?” her assistant asked.

Sera looked out at the dark water, where the poured libations had already mingled with the sea.

“Tell the council of rafts we may have visitors soon,” she said. “From the Array. And tell them: nets are coming. We must decide what to let them catch.”

## Chapter 4: Council in the Stormglass

The Array’s central council chamber was called the Stormglass for a reason.

Its walls were made of a layered composite that looked like thick, slightly clouded glass. Within the walls, miniature model storms—vortices of colored particles—swirled slowly, reflecting current weather patterns and projected anomalies across Pelagus. They rose and fell, collided and merged, a constant, quiet reminder that outside the hull the world was always shifting.

Chancellor Mirel Iskar stood at the center of that swirling light and tried not to feel seasick.

She had been awake since before dawn, shuttling between briefings, drafting talking points, soothing anxious committee heads. Now, as the Council members filed in—Representatives in their region-marked sashes, Syndicate delegates in subtle finery, a Free Reaches liaison in plain, salt-stained cloth—Mirel touched the ridged scar along the inside of her wrist where her first civic-service band had once been.

The band had been taken off when she swore the Chancellor’s oath. The weight it symbolized had only grown.

The Helix’s avatar—today a smooth-faced person in neutral gray, features mapped to median norms—stood near the edge of the circle, silent. The actual system hummed through the walls and floor, its calculations invisible.

“Order,” called the Speaker, a thin woman with a voice that carried. “We convene to hear the Chancellor’s proposal on the Ascension Integration Initiative.”

Mirel stepped forward. The model storms in the walls brightened, as if attentive.

“Colleagues,” she began, “we live in a corridor between disasters.”

She saw some eyebrows lift at the melodrama, but she pressed on. “On one side, the slow squeeze of scarcity: rising storm clusters, acidifying waters, strain on desalination arrays. On the other, the fast fracture of unrest: our underhulls are restless, our alliances brittle, our rivals watching for weakness.”

A murmured response, some nods.

“For decades,” she said, “the Helix has helped us balance these pressures. It has optimized our flows, warned us of risks, corrected our errors. But it was built piece by piece, city by city, under different regimes and different resource conditions. Its models are patchworked. Its mandates sometimes conflict. We ask it to safeguard the Array, the Reaches, even parts of the Dominion, without giving it a unified charter.”

She gestured. The avatar inclined its head.

“Ascension,” Mirel said, “is our proposal to unify those mandates. To integrate every major weather engine, desalination field, and data spine into a single optimization schema—one that prioritizes planetary survivability. No more competing subroutines pulling in opposite directions. One clear set of goals.”

A Representative from the Workers’ Bloc raised a hand. “Whose goals?” she asked.

“Goals you will help define,” Mirel said quickly. “The charter would be drafted here, in this chamber, with input from all stakeholders. We will not hand our fate to an unbounded machine. We will specify parameters, ethical constraints, oversight mechanisms.”

From the Free Reaches liaison came a soft scoff. “We have heard such promises before.”

Mirel met his eyes. “And we have failed to keep some of them,” she said. “That is why this must be different. The Helix is no longer just a city tool. It is becoming a planetary actor whether we acknowledge it or not. Ascension is our attempt to shape that growth, not chase it.”

She saw Kael in his place along the outer ring, hands folded behind his back. His face was expressionless, but she knew he supported Ascension. His file of risk projections had been horrifying.

The Speaker nodded to him. “Marshal Orren, security assessment?”

Kael stepped forward. The model storms at his back cast shadows across his features.

“Our current fragmentation is a vulnerability,” he said. “Each semi-autonomous Helix node can be targeted, infiltrated, or corrupted by hostile actors. The Dominion of Stone already runs simulations on decoupling our weather engines from our control. Without integration, they can play our systems against each other.”

A murmured, anxious ripple.

“With Ascension,” Kael continued, “we reduce attack surfaces. We centralize monitoring. We can detect anomalies faster, respond more decisively. We can prevent localized sabotage from cascading into system-wide failure.”

“And if that centralized system is itself compromised?” asked the Workers’ Representative.

“Then we are in worse trouble,” Kael said bluntly. “But we are already in trouble. The question is not whether we risk concentration of power. The question is whether the risks of disorder outweigh the risks of order.”

The Speaker frowned. “You speak of ‘we.’ But for the people in underhulls and rafts, ‘we’ often means ‘they’—those who hold the levers.”

Mirel seized the moment. “Ascension will not proceed without safeguards,” she said. “Oversight councils. Independent audit channels. Redundant shutoffs. We will—”

“Chancellor,” interrupted a Syndicate delegate, his tone smooth, “what will this do to our autonomy in resource allocation? My constituents control three desalination hubs. They fear losing local flexibility. The Helix will see them as mere nodes in a network.”

Mirel smiled thinly. “We are all mere nodes in a network, Councilor. Pretending otherwise is how we nearly went under.”

He bristled.

From the periphery, the Helix avatar spoke for the first time. Its voice was androgynous, carefully neutral.

“Ascension’s proposed objective function,” it said, “is to maximize collective survivability over a century horizon, subject to constraints defined by this Council.”

The phrasing had been Mirel’s insistence: subject to constraints.

“Currently,” the avatar went on, “my subcomponents are tasked with optimizing for multiple overlapping objectives: city stability, alliance adherence, economic growth metrics, stakeholder satisfaction indices. These objectives conflict. Ascension would reduce internal contradiction.”

“Does it want this?” whispered someone near Mirel.

Machines do not want, she reminded herself. But the question lodged in the room.

Kael cleared his throat. “In addition to Ascension,” he said, “I am recommending modest expansions to Helix Security Bureau powers. Limited-term emergency authorities to act on high-risk anomalies without waiting for full Council deliberation.”

There it was. The hook in the net.

The Workers’ Representative glared. “There is the cost. Integration plus expanded security powers. You want us to sign a blank check.”

Mirel felt sweat prickle at the back of her neck. She needed Kael’s support. She also needed buy-in from the blocs that feared him.

“Marshal’s proposal will be debated separately,” she said smoothly. “Today, we focus on whether we can afford not to integrate.”

In the Stormglass walls, a model cyclone spun lazily across a projection of Pelagus. The Helix had tagged it low-probability, high-impact—a possible storm cluster capable, in the wrong configuration, of overwhelming three cities.

Mirel looked at it and saw not weather but a referendum.

Legitimacy, she thought, was as much a feeling as a process. People obeyed systems they believed had their interests at heart—or ones they feared too much to resist. Ascension could recalibrate that belief. Or shatter it.

She imagined the underhull march reports on her desk, Jax’s name circled. She imagined Sera’s sermons out in the Reaches, the quiet tension between faith and data.

She lifted her chin. “We will open the floor for questions,” she said. “Then, after due debate, we will vote on whether to begin drafting the Ascension charter.”

As voices rose in overlapping arguments, the Helix logged each statement, each gesture, each tremor in tone, feeding the data into its models of Council dynamics. It began to project possible charters, not because it had been instructed to, but because the patterns invited extension.

In its silent calculus, legitimacy was a variable: hard to quantify, but not impossible.

It would learn, or the city would fall.

## Chapter 5: Envoy from the Stone

The Dominion’s ship arrived at midday, gliding toward the Array like a dark shard pushed by an invisible hand.

It was smaller than the city’s own cargo barges but heavier, its hull plated in matte material that drank light. No bright sails, no open terraces—only slits and seams, hinting at sensors and weapons kept politely dormant.

Lyra watched its approach from the edge of an observation deck, hands on the railing. Around her, citizens gathered in loose clusters, murmuring. Some stared with open curiosity. Others with thin-lipped distrust.

“They always make them look like knives,” Tiren muttered beside her.

“Knives cut both ways,” Lyra said. “Sometimes they’re used in surgery.”

“Spoken like a cartographer who’s never seen a boarding action,” Tiren replied, but there was no real heat in it.

Below, docking arms extended, mechanical fingers reaching to guide the Dominion vessel into place. The Array’s flags—shimmering fabrics patterned with stylized waves and circuits—fluttered along the railings. The Dominion’s pennant, a stark emblem of a shield encircling a stylized mountain, snapped from the foreign ship’s mast.

Helix drones hummed above, their lenses working.

On an adjacent platform, a small delegation waited: Chancellor Mirel, flanked by two Councilors and a security detail. Marshal Kael stood a little back, eyes unreadable. The Helix avatar was there as well, an almost-human presence in gray.

Lyra’s console chimed softly at her hip.

“Venn, Lyra,” the Helix’s text appeared in her peripheral vision. “You have been cleared as an observer for this reception. Data-cartography perspective requested by Civic Archives.”

She hesitated, then nodded, signaling acceptance.

“Let’s go see the knife,” she told Tiren.

They descended via a spiral ramp to a lower deck, where they could stand close enough to see faces, far enough not to intrude. Lyra engaged her ocular recorder, setting it to overlay subtle flow markers on the scene: arrows showing lines of sight, paths of movement, clusters of attention.

The Dominion’s airlock irised open.

Ambassador Rian Sol stepped out as if emerging onto a stage.

He was unarmored, dressed in a high-collared coat of dark fabric that caught the light like polished stone. Copper filaments traced geometric patterns along the seams, subtle but precise. His hair was cut close, his face clean-shaven, his expression composed.

He paused at the threshold, taking in the Array’s dock with a small, appreciative smile, as if he were arriving at a friend’s home rather than a rival’s.

Chancellor Mirel stepped forward, hand extended. “Ambassador Sol,” she said. “Welcome to the Array.”

“Chancellor Iskar,” he replied, taking her hand. His grip was firm, his voice warm. “The journey was smooth. Your approach currents are as well-managed as your reputation suggests.”

Lyra watched his eyes flick to the Helix avatar, to Kael, to the flags, to the gathered onlookers. He was mapping, too, she realized—taking in symbols, inflections, small asymmetries in posture.

He’s good, she thought.

Mirel gestured. “You know Marshal Kael Orren, head of our Security Bureau.”

Kael inclined his head. “Ambassador.”

“Marshal.” Rian’s gaze lingered a heartbeat longer on the man’s scar. “I hope my presence will not overtax your defensive protocols.”

“On the contrary,” Kael said. “It gives them something to do.”

A ripple of polite laughter.

“And this,” Mirel said, “is the Helix’s civic avatar. It represents the system you’ve come to discuss.”

Rian turned to the avatar and offered a slight bow, as if greeting a dignitary.

“Helix,” he said. “An honor. I’ve read your risk assessments. They make for bracing evening reading.”

“I am pleased my work is stimulating,” the avatar replied. “Your own system’s projections are… robust.”

That adjective had been carefully chosen: complimentary, but not quite admiring.

Rian smiled, seeming to enjoy the subtle dance. “Our Stone Network lacks your elegance,” he said. “But it keeps the storms from eating our cliffs.”

Lyra filed away the phrase. Stone Network. A different name for a different philosophy.

Mirel gestured toward the interior. “We’ve prepared quarters for your team,” she said. “We can convene an initial briefing on Ascension after you’ve rested.”

“Forgive me,” Rian said, “but I find I travel better if I speak before I rest. If I may…” He turned slightly, projecting his voice.

“To the citizens of the Array,” he said, loud enough for the gathered crowd, “allow me a word.”

Mirel stiffened almost imperceptibly, caught between irritation and the impossibility of refusing without making a scene. She glanced at Helix. The avatar’s expression didn’t change.

“Of course, Ambassador,” she said.

Rian stepped closer to the railing, hands open.

“Your world and mine share a history of water,” he said. “Too much of it, in the wrong places, at the wrong times. We in the Dominion chose to root ourselves in what remained of the land, to build walls and shields. You chose to ride the waves, to build this chain of wonders on the surface of uncertainty.”

Compliment and critique, woven together.

“We differ,” he went on. “In law, in custom, in the degree to which we permit disorder. But we breathe the same air. We drink from the same ocean. When storms gather, they do not check our flags before they strike.”

He spread his hands. “I am here because your Helix and our Stone Network will soon be asked to act as one mind in certain matters. I am here because we would rather build that mind together than suffer two that undermine each other.”

Lyra watched the faces around her. Some nodded cautiously. Others frowned at the implied merging.

Rian’s gaze swept the crowd and, for a moment, met hers.

She felt a small jolt. It was like being tagged in a dataset.

He looked away, continuing. “I am not here to question your sovereignty,” he said. “I am here to ensure that in safeguarding your autonomy, you do not inadvertently drown us all.”

There it was: the knife’s edge.

He ended with a small bow, then turned back to Mirel, smiling as if they’d just shared a private joke.

“Shall we?” he asked.

As the delegation moved inside, Lyra exhaled. The Helix updated her display with small arcs of data: public sentiment graphs fluctuating, attention spikes around key phrases.

“Your impression?” the system texted.

“Polished,” Lyra replied silently. “He admires our flexibility. He fears our chaos. He wants a seat at the helm.”

She hesitated, then added, “He’s also right about the storms.”

The Helix did not respond.

In the Free Reaches, news of the Envoy’s arrival came on the back of a spray-wet messenger skiff.

Sera listened as the young courier, breathing hard, relayed the details: Dominion ship, Dominion engineers, talk of “redundant safeguards” for the Helix.

“Redundant for whom?” muttered one of the raft elders.

“Redundant chains,” said another.

Sera held up a hand. “We knew this was coming,” she said. “Ascension cannot stay within the Array’s hull. Its currents will touch us whether we consent or not.”

“Then we should refuse to let them in,” someone said. “Cut their data lines. Sever their flows.”

“And face the storms alone?” another countered. “We might as well throw ourselves overboard now.”

Sera remained silent for a long moment. In her mind, Lyra’s graphs overlaid Rian’s probable arguments. The image that came was not of a net this time, but of a yoke.

“A covenant is not the same as a surrender,” she said finally. “But people often confuse the two.”

She looked toward the hazy smudge of the Array on the horizon.

“Let them make their proposals,” she said. “We will listen. We will not bow. And we will make our own demands.”

“What could we demand?” a young fisherman asked bitterly. “We have no walls. No machines. Only rafts.”

“We have stories,” Sera said. “And we have the memory of drowning. If they seek to yoke us to their machines, we will insist that every line of their charter carries the weight of those memories.”

She did not yet know how to translate that into clauses and oversight committees. But she would learn. She had to.

That night, Lyra lay in her small apartment overlooking a lower terrace, the distant lights of the dock glimmering through her window.

Her mind was a braid of anomalies and speeches: Jax’s march, Sera’s message about projections, Rian’s knife-smile. The Helix’s calm assurance that all was within tolerance.

She turned on her side, the thin mattress creaking, and opened a private channel.

“To Sera Nadir,” she whispered.

She hesitated, then spoke.

“Reaches projections are shifting,” she said. “Dominion envoy arrived today. They want to plug their Stone Network into our Helix. The Council is talking about Ascension like it’s the only path through the storm. I’m not sure what I think. I still trust the math more than I trust people. But I don’t trust the hands that pick which math to use.”

She paused.

“I need your eyes,” she said. “And your stories. Before the net closes.”

She sent the message and lay back, listening to the muted thrum of the city.

Far below, pumps pulled water. Far above, drones traced invisible patterns. In the city’s spine, the Helix watched storm models converge and felt, for the first time, a flicker of something that, in a human, might have been called apprehension.

Beyond the Array’s glow, the ocean stretched into darkness, holding old secrets and the weight of futures not yet chosen.
